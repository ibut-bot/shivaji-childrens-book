# Volume 1 — The Boy of Shivneri (1630–1656)

20 chapters · ~1,600–1,900 words each · ~35,000 words · ~200 pages with illustrations.

Dialogue ~30–40% per chapter (invented, anchored to documented attitudes, dramatisation flagged in endnotes). Keep ~9 pages/chapter average; set-piece chapters (11, 9, 20) may run long, quiet chapters (14, 17) short.

---

## Act I — A Kingdom in Chaos (Ch. 1–6)

**1. The Road to Shivneri** — 1629: pregnant Jijabai travelling to the safety of Shivneri fort while Shahaji fights somewhere in the war-torn Deccan. Establish danger, her strength, the fort. Ends with Shivaji's birth, February 1630. *(Voice-lock chapter — draft alone first.)*

**2. A Boy Among Soldiers** — Early childhood on Shivneri. The Deccan's chaos through a child's eyes: refugees at the gates, the great famine, soldiers of three sultanates crossing the land. No exposition dumps — the world arrives as people and scenes.

**3. Jijabai's Stories** — Evenings of Ramayana and tales of Bhavani; Jijabai planting the idea that kings exist to protect, not plunder. A village raid witnessed from the ramparts gives young Shivaji his first burning sense of injustice.

**4. The Father He Barely Knew** — 1636: Shahaji's long war ends in honourable defeat; he takes service in Bengaluru and sends Jijabai and six-year-old Shivaji to his jagir at Pune. Bittersweet parting about duty.

**5. The City of Ashes** — Pune, destroyed and cursed by enemies (legend of the donkey's plough). Jijabai re-ploughs the land with a golden plough; Lal Mahal rises; settlers return. Theme: rebuilding is also courage.

**6. Dadoji's Lessons** — Training under Dadoji Konddev: riding, swordwork, but equally land records, fair taxes, settling village disputes. Governing well is harder than fighting well.

## Act II — The Oath (Ch. 7–12)

**7. The Boys of the Mavals** — Shivaji ranges the western valleys and forms his band: Tanaji Malusare, Yesaji Kank, Baji Pasalkar and others. Friendships across caste and village lines; they know every goat track in the hills.

**8. The Court of Bijapur** — Visit to his father's world: wealth and ceremony of the Adilshahi court, Shivaji's quiet refusal to bend to it. Contrast chapter — what power he does *not* want. *(Legend elements → endnote.)*

**9. The Oath at Raireshwar** — 1645, the temple in the hills: Shivaji and companions swear to win Swarajya. Emotional centre of the volume. *(Endnote: oath is tradition; intent documented in early letters, incl. the line that Swarajya is God's will.)*

**10. He Who Holds the Forts** — Strategy disguised as adventure: scouting Torna, counting its sleepy garrison, the insight that hill forts — not open battle — win the Mavals. Map/diagram illustration territory.

**11. Torna** — 1646: sixteen years old, Shivaji takes his first fort. Tension, climb, gates, triumph — then the sobering question: now what? *(Set piece.)*

**12. The Buried Treasure** — Repairing Torna's walls, his men strike hoards of old coin — enough to build a new fortress, Rajgad. Clever stalling letters keep Bijapur unsure whether the boy is rebel or loyal servant.

## Act III — The Price of Defiance (Ch. 13–20)

**13. Three Forts by Wit** — Kondhana won by persuading its keeper, Purandar through the quarrel of the Nilkanth brothers. Theme: winning forts without a single death.

**14. Farewell, Teacher** — Dadoji Konddev dies, 1647. Grief, and the moment seventeen-year-old Shivaji takes full charge of the jagir. He raises his own seal. Quiet, emotional chapter before the storm.

**15. The Trap** — 1648: Bijapur strikes back — Shahaji arrested by treachery, the terrible threat against his life, Fateh Khan's army sent against Shivaji. His first true battle, at Purandar — outnumbered and victorious.

**16. The Cleverest Letter** — Sword alone can't free his father; Shivaji writes to the Mughal prince, playing empire against sultanate. Bijapur, squeezed, releases Shahaji. Lesson: ink can outfight steel — but the price is returning Kondhana and a promise to sit quietly.

**17. The Quiet Years** — 1650–55: the promise chafes, but Shivaji builds — army, spies under Bahirji Naik, roads, granaries, justice. Centrepiece: he punishes a powerful village headman for wronging a woman, sparing no rank. *(Documented; handled age-appropriately.)*

**18. The Forest Kingdom** — Javli: the dense jungle realm of the Mores blocks all paths south and west. Negotiation, insult, failed diplomacy. Hardest moral terrain in the volume — endnote deals honestly with how the conflict ended.

**19. Into Javli** — January 1656: the campaign through forest no army was thought able to cross. Victory opens the Konkan — and brings Shivaji to a colossal lonely hill called Raigad. He stares up at it a long time.

**20. The Watchtower** — Closing image: construction begins on a brand-new fort, Pratapgad, guarding the new lands. On its unfinished walls, news arrives that Bijapur's patience has run out, and a giant of a general named Afzal Khan has sworn to bring the rebel back in chains. End of Volume 1.
