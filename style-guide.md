# Style Guide — Shivaji Series

The voice bible. Every chapter inherits these rules. Update only after a deliberate decision (note the date and reason).

## Reader
Ages 8–12. Confident readers but not adults. Light, fast, immersive. They love adventure, fairness, secret plans, maps, forts, and clever underdogs.

## Voice
- **Third person, past tense, limited-ish to Shivaji** (we can briefly sit with Jijabai in early chapters before Shivaji is old enough to carry POV).
- Warm, vivid, propulsive. A storyteller's voice — like it's being read aloud at bedtime.
- Sentences mostly short to medium. Vary rhythm; let a short punchy sentence land after a longer one.
- **Show, don't tell.** Reveal feelings and stakes through action, dialogue, sensory detail — not narrator summary. ("Jijabai's hands did not shake as she folded the letter" not "Jijabai was brave.")

## Structure of every chapter
- **Hook** in the first paragraph — drop the reader into motion, a question, or an image.
- Clear **beginning / middle / end** — a small arc that resolves *something* even as the larger story continues.
- **End with a forward pull** — a line that makes the reader want the next chapter. Not a cliffhanger every time; sometimes a quiet promise.
- Aim 1,600–1,900 words. Dialogue ~30–40%.

## Dialogue
- Natural, period-flavoured but not archaic or stiff. No "thee/thou," no fake-formal "Indeed, my liege."
- Each major character has a consistent voice (track in `continuity.md`). Tanaji blunt and warm; Jijabai steady and principled; Dadoji dry and exacting; young Shivaji curious then increasingly resolute.
- Use dialogue to carry exposition wherever possible instead of narrator info-dumps.

## Vocabulary
- **Ceiling:** a bright 12-year-old should never need a dictionary twice on a page. Stretch words are fine occasionally if context carries them.
- Prefer concrete to abstract. "The grain ran out" beats "famine ensued."

## Marathi & historical terms
- Weave in inline, made clear by context on first use; full definitions live in the glossary.
- Italicise on first appearance only (e.g. *Swarajya*, *jagir*, *killedar*, *Maval*, *waghnakh*).
- Don't over-salt a page with untranslated terms — two or three new ones per chapter max.
- Names: Shivaji, Jijabai, Shahaji, Dadoji Konddev, Tanaji Malusare, Yesaji Kank, Baji Pasalkar, Bahirji Naik, Afzal Khan. Use "Shivaji" in narration (not "Shivaji Maharaj" until/unless crowned in later volumes).

## Sensitivity (mirrors CLAUDE.md — do not drift)
- Theme is **Swarajya, justice, protection of ordinary people of all faiths.** Never communal.
- Antagonists are individuals (a cruel official, a treacherous general), driven by ambition or injustice — not a people or a religion.
- **Violence:** stakes and consequence, not gore. Cut away from the blow. Death is felt through grief and aftermath, not depicted graphically.

## "What We Know" endnotes
- Short, friendly, curious — "Here's what historians are sure about, and here's the part that's a famous story." Builds trust and educational value (school/library sales).
- Flag: invented dialogue (once, generally), legends, and any compressed/rearranged events.

## Illustration hooks (for later phase)
- While drafting, naturally include one strong **full-page illustration moment** per chapter and one **spot-art** subject (map, fort cross-section, seal, weapon, object). Don't label them in the prose — just make sure the visual beat exists. We'll extract these later.
