Chhatrapati Shivaji · Volume 1 · The Boy of Shivneri

Chapter 17 — The Quiet Years

Who & Where in this chapter

Characters

Shivaji had promised to sit still, and it was very nearly killing him.

For a young man with a vow burning in his chest and a map full of forts he did not yet hold, doing nothing was the hardest order he had ever been given — and he had given it to himself, as the price of his father's life. So for five long years he kept it. He took no new forts. He fought no battles. To the watchers in Bijapur, the troublesome Bhonsle boy had finally settled down into a quiet, obedient nobleman.

They should have looked closer.

Because Shivaji had discovered something about sitting still: if you cannot grow outward, you can grow deeper. And in those five quiet years, without a single banner raised in war, he built the thing that would one day shake the whole Deccan.

He built an army — really built it. Not a band of hill boys with country swords anymore, but companies of trained, disciplined soldiers, drilled in the valleys, paid in fair and regular coin from his own treasury so that no man of his ever needed to rob a village to eat. He strengthened his forts, quietly, a wall here and a water tank there, nothing that looked like rebellion, everything that would matter when rebellion came.

He governed, the way Dadoji had taught him — fair fixed taxes written down so no official could cheat a farmer, roads mended, granaries filled, disputes settled honestly. Under his hand the Maval country grew prosperous and safe, and the people noticed, the way people always notice whether the man above them is a roof or a storm.

And he built something no one had ever quite built before: eyes.

His name was Bahirji Naik, and you would never have looked at him twice.

That was the entire point. He was a lean, ordinary, forgettable man who could become anyone — a wandering singer, a holy beggar, a trader of cloth, a simple traveller on a dusty road — and walk straight into any fort, any town, any enemy camp in the Deccan, and walk out again knowing everything. How many soldiers. Which gate was weak. When the pay was late and the men were grumbling. What the great lords said when they thought no one was listening.

Bahirji built Shivaji a web of such people, spread invisibly across the whole country, and through it Shivaji came to see — to know his enemies' business better than they knew it themselves. An army can be beaten by a bigger army. But an army that is always surprised, always a step behind, that never knows where the blow will fall until it has already fallen — that army can be beaten by a much smaller one.

"They have iron," Shivaji said. "We'll have eyes. We'll see them coming for years before they see us."

It was, like the forts, a patient weapon. And Shivaji was, it turned out, learning patience at last.

But the quiet years are best remembered for something else — something that had nothing to do with armies or spies, and everything to do with what all of it was for.

There was a village headman, a patil, a powerful and well-connected man, and he did a terrible thing. He wronged a young woman — a poor farmer's daughter, a girl with no rank and no powerful family to protect her — cruelly, and badly, in the way that powerful men had been wronging the powerless since the beginning of time. And he did it without a moment's fear, because he was a patil, and she was nobody, and everyone knew how this story always went. The mighty did as they pleased. The small swallowed it. That was simply the world.

The case came before Shivaji.

He did not wave it away, the way a hundred lords before him would have. He did not quietly take the powerful man's side. He listened — to the broken family, to the witnesses — and he had it investigated carefully, and patiently, and completely, until the truth was beyond any doubt.

And then he brought the entire weight of his justice down on the headman's head, and he did not soften it by a single ounce because the man was powerful.

The patil lost everything — his post, his lands, his standing — and he was punished with a severity that rang through every village in the country. The lesson was deliberate, and Shivaji meant every word of it: in his land, rank would not buy you mercy for a crime like this. A poor girl's life and honour weighed exactly as much as a great man's — more, because she was the one who had been wronged, and protecting her was the whole point of having a king at all.

"What is the use of forts and armies," he said, "if a farmer's daughter cannot sleep safe in her own village? That is what we are building. Never forget it. The strong protecting the weak — not feeding on them. If we ever become the kind of power that looks away from this, then we are just one more storm, and we should never have begun."

Word of it spread faster than any victory could have.

In village after village, ordinary people — farmers, herders, craftsmen, the small and the overlooked — heard that there was, at last, a lord in the hills who actually meant it. Who would protect them. Who held the mighty to the same law as the meek, and came down hardest of all on those who preyed on the helpless. They had been promised such kings only in Jijabai's old stories. Now there was one, in stone and flesh, a few valleys away.

And that — more than the forts, more than the spies, more than the trained companies in the valleys — was the true thing Shivaji built in his quiet years. He built love. The fierce, rooted loyalty of a whole people who had decided that this was their king, and his cause was their cause. An empire can buy soldiers. It cannot buy that. And it cannot, however hard it tries, defeat it.

Five years. An army, drilled and waiting. A web of eyes across the Deccan. A country governed so fairly that its people would die before they gave it up. A vow, banked like a fire, burning lower but never out.

The boy who could not sit still had used his stillness to become something far more dangerous than a restless boy: a patient one.

And now the patience was running out — in the best way. The promise he had made to Bijapur was wearing thin with the years, and his eyes were lifting again to the map, to the one direction his growing little kingdom had never been able to go. West and south, where the roads to the sea were blocked by a wild, forested mountain realm ruled by a proud and dangerous clan who bowed to no one.

If Swarajya was ever going to be more than a handful of hill forts, that door had to open.

It was called Javli. And it was not going to open easily.

What We Know

True: The years from about 1650 to 1655 were indeed a relatively quiet period in which Shivaji consolidated — strengthening his army and forts and governing his territory with a fairness that genuinely won the people's loyalty. Bahirji Naik was a real and famous figure: the head of Shivaji's remarkable network of spies and scouts, whose intelligence-gathering became one of the great secret weapons of the Maratha state. And Shivaji's reputation for strict, impartial justice — especially in fiercely protecting women and ordinary people, and punishing offenders no matter how highly ranked — is very well documented. The case in this chapter is based on a real one: a village patil (Babaji Gujar of Ranje) who committed a grave crime against a young woman and whom Shivaji punished severely, stripping him of rank and lands and sparing him nothing for his high position.

Story: We've kept the crime deliberately undescribed — it was an ugly, violent wrong, and this is a book for young readers — but it is historically real, as is Shivaji's unflinching response (by the harsh standards of the 1600s, the punishment was very severe). The dialogue is, as always, an imagined dramatisation of his documented values. The five years are compressed into a single picture of the kind of building he did.

Words in this chapter: patil (a village headman or chief).
