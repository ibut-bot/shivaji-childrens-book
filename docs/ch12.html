Chapter 12 — The Buried Treasure

The morning after you take a fort, it turns out, you do not feel like a conqueror. You feel like a man with a very large, very broken house and no money to fix it.

Shivaji walked the walls of Torna in the cold light of that first real morning and made a list in his head, and the list was not glorious. The gate would not shut properly. Whole stretches of rampart had slumped into rubble where the rains had got into them year after year. The great water tank was half choked with leaves. There was grain enough in the stores for his men for perhaps a month, if the rats took their fair share and no more, which rats never did.

"We took the proudest fort in the country," Tanaji said, coming up beside him, looking at the same sad wall. "And it's falling down."

"It's been falling down for years," Shivaji said. "Nobody noticed, because nobody cared. That was the whole reason we could walk in." He toed a loose stone back into a gap, where it sat for a moment and then fell out again. "Now we care. So now we have to fix it. All of it. Before Bijapur remembers it owns a mountain."

Tanaji groaned. "With what? We've got swords and good legs and about eleven coins between the lot of us."

"I know," Shivaji said. "Start digging anyway."

So they became builders.

It was not what any of them had imagined when they swore an oath in a cold temple to free a kingdom. They had pictured battles, banners, glory — not this: hauling stone up goat tracks, mixing mortar, clearing the muck of decades out of a water tank with their bare hands, while their young leader argued with old masons from the valley villages about how a wall should be footed so it would stand another hundred years.

But Shivaji worked alongside them, his fine clothes as filthy as anyone's, and a strange thing happened, the same thing that had happened around the fires: men will give you everything if you bleed and sweat the same as they do. The wall began, slowly, to rise.

And then, one ordinary afternoon, a man's pick went into the earth at the foot of an old tower and came back with a clonk that was not the sound of stone.

He called for Shivaji before he even finished digging it out.

What they pulled from the ground, as the whole work-party gathered round in disbelieving silence, was a fat clay pot, its mouth sealed with wax gone hard as rock — and when they broke it open, the afternoon sun fell into it and came back gold.

Coins. Old coins, hundreds of them, the small thick gold pieces men called hons, buried who knew how long ago by who knew whom — some forgotten commander, some frightened treasurer, some king's clerk in a year nobody now remembered, hiding the pay-chest against a war that had swept over this mountain and never given anyone the chance to come back for it.

And it was not the only pot.

They dug all that evening and into the night by torchlight, and they kept finding them — pot after pot of old buried gold, more money than any boy in that work-party had ever seen or dreamed of, more money than their whole valleys put together had passed through their hands in their lifetimes.

Tanaji sat down hard on the freshly turned earth and laughed, a little wildly. "We're rich," he said. "Shivaji. We're rich."

"We're funded," Shivaji said, and there was a difference in the way he said it, and the men nearest him caught it and went quiet.

Because he was already looking past the gold, the way he always looked past things, to what the gold could become. And it was not feasting, and it was not fine clothes, and it was not a single thing for himself.

It was walls.

"This much," he told them later, turning a single hon over and over in his fingers, "doesn't make us safe. It makes us able. There's a difference, and you should learn it now, because it's the most important thing buried in that ground, and it isn't the gold."

Torna they had taken because it was old and forgotten and weak. Now, a few valleys away, Shivaji had his eye on another hill — a great sprawling mountain called Murumbdev, with room on its broad shoulders for everything a real stronghold needed. Not a fort patched up from someone else's ruin. A fort built from the first stone his way, by his own people, belonging to no sultan who had ever lived.

He would pour the buried gold into it. He would raise walls along its ridges and a strong citadel — a balekilla — on its highest point, and storehouses that stayed full and tanks that stayed clean and gates that actually shut. And he would give it a new name, because it was going to be the first true seat of the thing he had sworn to build.

He called it Rajgad. The King of Forts.

There was, of course, one problem with raising a brand-new fortress and patching up a stolen one, which was that it is very difficult to do quietly, and Bijapur was not going to stay forgetful forever. Sooner or later a report would reach the great marble court in the south: the son of Shahaji has seized Torna and is building a fort of his own.

Tanaji, who solved most problems by hitting them, did not see a way around this one. "When they hear, they'll send an army," he said. "You said so yourself, on the wall."

"They will," Shivaji agreed. "Eventually. So our job is to make 'eventually' as far away as we can manage." He smiled, the particular thin smile. "We can't beat their army today. But we can confuse the men who decide whether to send it."

And so the boy who had just stolen a mountain sat down and wrote the sultan's officers a very polite letter.

It was a small masterpiece. In it, Shivaji was the most loyal, most dutiful servant the Adilshahi had ever been blessed with. Torna? He had not stolen Torna — heaven forbid. He had merely secured a neglected royal fort that lazy, dishonest local men had let fall into ruin, taking it into safe hands for the good of His Majesty's realm. The new fort? Why, simply strengthening the frontier in His Majesty's name. He was, he assured them, their humblest and most obedient servant, and he signed it with every flowery courtesy the court could wish for.

It was not exactly true. It was not exactly a lie. It was something far more useful: a fog.

Tanaji read it over Shivaji's shoulder, moving his lips, and his honest face slowly screwed up in confusion.

"But," he said, "this says you're on their side."

"Mm."

"You're not on their side. We swore an oath that you're the opposite of on their side."

"I know what we swore." Shivaji folded the letter and pressed it shut. "And one day they'll know it too. But not today, Tanaji. Today, somewhere far away, a tired official is going to read this, and frown, and not be quite sure. Is this Bhonsle boy a rebel? Or just an overeager young servant who took some initiative? Is he worth the enormous bother and expense of sending an army into these miserable hills to find out?" He set the letter aside for the messenger. "And while he's frowning and not being sure — our walls go up another foot."

Tanaji thought about this for a long moment. "That," he admitted, "is sneaky."

"That," said Shivaji, "is time. The sword isn't the only weapon. Sometimes the best thing you can do to an enemy is leave him standing there, scratching his head, while you get ready." He looked out across the hills toward the great hump of Murumbdev — toward Rajgad, rising stone by stone out of the buried gold of a forgotten war. "We won't be ready for a long while yet. So we keep them guessing for a long while yet."

The walls went up. The letters went south, polite and frequent and faintly bewildering. And in the marble halls of Bijapur, for now, the frown won out over the army — which was exactly, precisely, what the boy in the hills had been counting on.

It would not last forever. Fog never does.

But it would last long enough.

What We Know

True: It is solid history that, right after taking Torna, Shivaji built up a major new fort nearby on the hill then called Murumbdev, renaming it Rajgad ("the King of Forts") — and that Rajgad became his capital for around twenty-six years, long before Raigad. It is also well documented that in these early years Shivaji repeatedly sent conciliatory, professedly loyal messages to the Bijapur (Adilshahi) court, presenting his fort-taking as faithful service rather than rebellion — buying time while he grew too strong to easily crush. This mix of the lion and the fox — winning by patience and cleverness, not only by the sword — is absolutely true to him.

Story (from the chronicles): The much-loved tale that Shivaji's men dug up buried treasure at Torna, which paid for building Rajgad, comes from the old Maratha chronicles (bakhars). It may well have a real core, but it was written down long after and the details (the pots, the amounts) are traditional rather than certain — so we've told it here as the chronicles tell it. The specific conversations and the wording of the letter are, as ever, an illustrative dramatisation of a well-attested strategy.

Words in this chapter: hon (a small thick gold coin of the Deccan), Rajgad ("the King of Forts"), balekilla (the strong inner citadel at a fort's highest point).
