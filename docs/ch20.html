Chapter 20 — The Watchtower

On a bare high ridge above the newly won country, a mountain was growing that had not been there the year before.

Not the kind of mountain the earth pushes up over a thousand years. This one was rising course by course, stone on dressed stone, under the hands of hundreds of working men — a fort being born. From dawn to dusk the ridge rang with it: the chock of hammers, the grind of stone on stone, the calls of the overseers, the long lines of men passing rough-cut blocks up the slope hand to hand. Where there had been only wind and grass, there were now the first proud courses of a rampart, curving along the cliff-edge exactly where the land wanted a wall to be.

Shivaji stood in the middle of it with white dust on his clothes and a great gladness in his chest, and watched his fort come up out of the ground.

"Pratapgad," he said, trying the name in his mouth. The fort of valour. He had chosen the name himself.

He had wanted this one to be entirely his.

Torna he had taken from a sleeping garrison; Kondhana and Purandar he had won by wit; Rajgad he had built up from an older hill. But Pratapgad he was raising from almost nothing, on ground he had chosen, to a plan he had argued over for weeks with the careful, clever man he had set in charge of the work — Moropant Trimbak Pingle, who could look at a naked ridge and see finished walls.

"Here," Shivaji had told him, walking the bare ground. "It has to be here. Look where the roads run."

And Moropant had looked, and seen what his master saw. For the ridge stood like a watchman over everything the long Javli campaign had won — the forest passes, the new country, the long roads tilting down toward the Konkan and the far bright sea. Whoever held this height held the gate to all of it. A fort here was not a place to hide. It was a watchtower over a kingdom.

"A roof," Shivaji murmured, half to himself, "over all of it."

He had been a baby in a fortress of black stone, born behind seven gates while the Deccan burned. Now he was a man building fortresses of his own, and he understood at last the thing his mother had told him on a rampart before he could even walk — that the small people needed something to stand under when the sky turned to fire. He was building it. Stone by stone, fort by fort, he was building it.

Jijabai came up to the unfinished walls one evening, leaning on the arm of a servant, her hair grey now under her shawl. She stood beside her son and looked out the way he was looking, west, toward the dropping light and the distant haze where the land ran down to the sea.

For a while neither of them spoke. They had stood like this before, the two of them, on another wall, a long time ago.

"You were born in a fort," she said at last. "Now you grow them like wheat." There was something in her voice that was very close to wonder.

"You told me once," Shivaji said, "that a wall is only stones until someone makes it a wall. That a person is the same. That if you keep choosing, and keep standing, one day you wake up and you are a fort." He smiled. "I have been trying to be a fort my whole life, Aai. It seemed only fair to build a few as well."

Jijabai laughed — a real laugh, rare and bright. Then she put her hand flat against the rough new stone of the parapet, as if she could feel a heartbeat in it.

"A vow," she said softly, "turned into a mountain. You did it." She looked at him. "The boy who said someone should make a kingdom where this does not happen. You remember?"

"I remember that you told me to remember I said it."

"Then this is the kingdom," she said. "It is small, and it is young, and it is made of borrowed hills and hard winters and men who love you. But it is real. It has a name now. Swarajya. Our own rule." Her eyes were wet, and she did not hide it, because there was no one she needed to be strong for on this wall tonight. "Whatever comes, they cannot say it was never built. We built it."

Whatever comes. Even as she said it, neither of them quite knew how soon the whatever would come.

It came as words, the way the worst news always reached him first — carried up the half-built ramp by one of Bahirji Naik's quiet, dusty, ordinary-looking men, who had ridden hard from the south.

Far away in Bijapur, the patience of the sultanate had finally, completely run out.

For years they had watched the Bhonsle boy and told themselves he was a nuisance who would tire, a rash youth who would overreach and break himself. Instead he had taken fort after fort, raised his own seal, freed his father out from under their noses, and now swallowed all of Javli and walked out onto the edge of the sea. He was not a nuisance anymore. He was a rebel kingdom, sitting in their hills, growing.

And so the court had resolved to end him. And to do it, the spy reported, they had chosen their most feared sword — a commander of enormous size and enormous reputation, a giant of a man who had crushed every enemy the sultanate had ever pointed him at, and who had stood in the great hall and sworn, before the whole court, that he would not come back without the rebel Shivaji — alive in chains, or dead across his saddle.

His name was Afzal Khan.

The spy told them what he knew, and it was not comforting. Afzal Khan was a mountain of a man, they said — tall and broad and immensely strong, a head above other men, with a voice like a war-drum and a long list of beaten enemies behind him. He had fought across the whole Deccan and lost very little. He was clever, too, in the hard way of a man who had spent his life winning, and he did not think much of a hill-chief barely past twenty who hid behind rocks and walls. He had taken the commission gladly. He meant to be quick about it. He had, the report said, already begun gathering a great army — cavalry and cannon and elephants, the full weight of Bijapur — to come up into the hills and pull this troublesome boy out of his forts like a badger out of a hole.

The name went around the unfinished walls of Pratapgad like a cold wind, and Shivaji watched it land on his men.

He saw Tanaji's jaw tighten. He saw the younger soldiers go quiet and still, the way men do when a shadow they have only heard about turns out to be real and to be coming. He had built a great deal in ten years — an army, a web of eyes, forts on a dozen hills, the love of a whole people. But he had never been hunted by a giant before, and neither had they.

"How big an army?" someone asked.

"Big," said the spy honestly. "Bigger than anything we have faced."

For a moment, no one said anything at all. The hammers had stopped for the evening, and the new fort stood half-made against the darkening sky, scaffolding and bare stone and open gaps where the gates would one day hang.

Then Shivaji laughed — not a frightened laugh, and not a foolish one. A low, almost glad laugh, the laugh of a man who has been preparing for a particular knock at the door for a very long time and has just heard it.

"Well," he said, and put his hand on the rough new parapet, exactly where his mother's hand had rested. "Now we find out what all of this was for."

He looked around at their faces, and his own was calm.

"They are sending their biggest storm," he said. "Let them. We did not spend ten years climbing mountains and learning every goat-track in these hills to be afraid of a man on a horse, however large the horse. He will have to come to us — into our forests, up our slopes, under our walls. And here," he said, and the unfinished fort seemed to lean in to listen, "here, we are home."

The work on Pratapgad went on into the night by torchlight, faster now, the men carrying stone with a new fierceness in their arms. Up out of the ridge it rose, the watchtower over the new kingdom, racing the storm that was already turning, far to the south, to look north into the hills.

A giant was coming for the boy of Shivneri.

But the boy of Shivneri was not a boy anymore. He was a builder of forts and a keeper of his people, a man who had turned a child's furious wish into stone and steel and Swarajya — and he was waiting for the giant on a mountain of his own making, in the heart of the wild country he knew better than any man alive.

What happened when the giant arrived — how Afzal Khan came up into these hills certain he was the hunter, and how he learned, too late, who the lion of the mountains truly was — is the next part of the story.

But that is the beginning of another book.

What We Know

True: Shivaji really did build the fort of Pratapgad (the name means roughly "fort of valour") soon after the conquest of Javli, around 1656, precisely to guard the newly won forest country and the routes toward the Konkan coast. The work was overseen for him by his able minister-engineer Moropant Trimbak Pingle (later one of the great ministers of his government). And Afzal Khan was a very real and very formidable Bijapur general — a famously large, powerful, and successful commander — whom the Adilshahi court did send to crush Shivaji, vowing to bring him to heel.

About the timing: We've placed Afzal Khan's threat at the close of this book as the storm on the horizon, because that is exactly what he was. The actual campaign and the legendary face-to-face meeting between the two men at the foot of Pratapgad happened a little later, in 1659 — which is where the next volume, The Mountain Lion, begins. So think of this last chapter as the gathering of the clouds, not the storm itself. The fort Shivaji is building here would, just three years on, become the stage for one of the most famous encounters in his whole life.

Story: The construction scene, the evening on the walls with Jijabai, and the arrival of the news are dramatised — built from documented facts (the fort, its purpose, its builder, the coming of Afzal Khan) and from the attitudes and relationships established across this volume, but the dialogue is, as always, imagined rather than recorded.

Words in this chapter: Pratapgad ("the fort of valour," the new fort guarding the Javli country); Swarajya (self-rule — "our own kingdom," the idea at the heart of the whole story).
