Chapter 9 — The Oath at Raireshwar

Who & Where in this chapter

Places

They climbed to the temple before dawn, while the hills were still black against a sky just beginning to think about light, because what they had come to do was not the kind of thing you did where anyone could see.

There were not many of them. A handful of young men, breathing hard from the climb, their breath smoking in the cold — Shivaji, and the closest of his Maval companions, the ones who had sat at the fires and asked whose hills are these, really. They had come a long way up, far from any town, far from any sultan's officer, to a place where there was nothing above them but the mountain and the sky and the old, old god who lived at the top.

The temple of Raireshwar stood on its high plateau the way it had stood for centuries: small, weathered, made of plain dark stone, with the cold wind moving through it. Inside, in the dark, was a lingam — the simple stone form of Shiva, the great god of the mountains. No gold. No jewelled throne. No rows of bowing nobles. Just stone, and a single oil lamp, and a god who had watched these hills since long before any sultan had ever heard of them.

It was, Shivaji had decided, exactly the right place.

He had thought for a long time about what he was going to say, and in the end he said it simply.

"You all know why we're here." His voice was quiet in the small stone room, but it did not shake. "We've talked around it for years. Tonight we stop talking around it."

The boys were very still. The lamp threw their shadows huge on the walls — and Shivaji, who had watched shadows on walls all his life and known they were never just shadows, felt the whole weight of his childhood standing behind him in the dark. His mother's stories. The burning village. The hungry boy who broke his bread in half. His father bowing low in a hall of gold.

"I have seen what they call greatness," he said. "I stood in the richest court in the Deccan and watched the greatest men I know crawl on a carpet for the right to keep what was already theirs. That is not greatness. That is a collar, made of gold so that the men who wear it won't notice it's a collar." He looked around at them, at each face. "I don't want it. I don't want to be the finest dog in someone else's house. And I don't think you do either."

"No," said Tanaji, low. The others echoed it. No.

"Then we want the other thing," Shivaji said. "The thing there isn't really even a proper word for, because nobody's been allowed to want it for so long. Our own. Our own land, ruled by us, for the people who actually live on it. Where a farmer keeps his own harvest. Where a village that's burning can call for help and someone comes. Where we bow"—and here he turned, and looked at the dark stone of the god—"to that, and to nothing else made of flesh that calls itself our master."

There was a word for it, old and almost forgotten. Swarajya. Self-rule. One's own.

What happened next, in that cold temple before the dawn, is the story the Marathas have told and treasured ever since — the most beloved scene in all of Shivaji's life. And like the most beloved stories, it has been told so many times, and polished so smooth and bright by love, that no one can now say exactly how much of it happened just so. We tell it here as it has always been told.

They say that Shivaji stepped up to the lingam, to the plain stone form of the god, and made his vow out loud — to win Swarajya, to free this land, to spend his whole life building a kingdom where the small and the poor and the unimportant would be protected, and would kneel to no foreign throne. They say that to seal it he offered not gold and not grand words but his own blood, a few drops of it, pressed to the cold stone — the most serious promise a person can make, paid in the only currency that is truly your own.

And they say that one by one, in the dark, his companions did the same. Tanaji. Yesaji. The hill boys who knew every goat track and owned almost nothing and had just decided to give what little they had — their strength, their loyalty, their lives if it came to it — to a boy of fifteen and an idea that the whole world would have called impossible.

When they came out of the temple, the sun was coming up over the edge of the world, and the hills were turning gold, and nothing at all had changed — they had no army, no forts, no money, no chance, by any sensible reckoning. And everything had changed, because a thing that had only been a feeling around a fire was now a vow, sworn before God, with blood on the stone to prove it.

Shivaji believed, for the rest of his life, that this was not only his own wish but something larger — that a land where ordinary people were protected and free was a thing the divine itself wanted. Years later he would put it into a letter, in words people still remember: that Swarajya — this self-rule he had sworn to win — was the will of God.

He was fifteen years old. He had a few dozen friends and a borrowed sense of destiny and a god of stone for a witness.

It should not have been nearly enough. Across the whole Deccan, in their domed cities and their golden halls, the great sultans and their generals would have laughed until they wept if they had heard that a teenaged boy in a cold hill-temple had just sworn to take their world apart and build a fairer one.

They would not be laughing for very long.

Because a vow, Shivaji knew even then, is only a beautiful noise until you do something about it. And self-rule, before it can be anything else, needs one plain and stubborn thing to stand on: ground. Land you actually hold. Walls that are actually yours.

It needs a fort.

And the boy who had spent his whole life learning these hills knew exactly, by now, which one he meant to take first.

What We Know

True: It is well documented that as a teenager in the mid-1640s, Shivaji genuinely committed himself to Swarajya — self-rule — and began gathering the Maval followers and the resolve that would launch his whole life's work. His belief that this cause was just, and even God-willed, is real: in his own early letters he expressed that Swarajya was the will of God, a line still quoted today. Raireshwar is a real, ancient hilltop temple of Shiva in the Sahyadri mountains.

Story (tradition): The famous, deeply loved scene of the oath at Raireshwar — the secret gathering, the vow before the god, the drops of blood on the stone — is cherished tradition, not an event recorded in detail at the time. We've told it here, lovingly, as the story it has always been. What is solid underneath it is just as stirring: a fifteen-year-old, in those very years, really did set his heart on the freedom of ordinary people and never once turned back.

Words in this chapter: Swarajya (self-rule; one's own kingdom), lingam (the simple stone form in which the god Shiva is worshipped).
