Chapter 4 — The Father He Barely Knew

Who & Where in this chapter

Objects

For the first years of his life, Shivaji's father was a name at the bottom of a letter.

He knew it was a great name. Shahaji Bhonsle was a soldier, and soldiers far away spoke it with respect. But the man himself was always somewhere beyond the hills, fighting a war that had been going on for as long as Shivaji had been alive. And in 1636, when Shivaji was six, that war finally ended.

It did not end well.

For years Shahaji had been trying to do something close to impossible. The old kingdom of Ahmadnagar — the Nizamshahi — was crumbling under the weight of the Mughal Empire pressing down from the north. Its sultan was gone and its armies were broken. Shahaji had taken a boy of the royal line, set him up as a new sultan, and fought, fort by fort and season by season, to keep a dying kingdom alive against the strongest power in India.

It was a brave thing to attempt. It was also a losing one. The Mughals were simply too many. One by one the strongholds fell, until at last Shahaji, holding out in the hills, ran out of choices an honourable soldier could take. He made terms, and laid down the fight.

A soldier who loses his war does not get to go home and rest. He has to find a new master and serve.

So Shahaji did a hard, ordinary, necessary thing. He took service with Bijapur — the rival sultanate he himself had often fought — because Bijapur was powerful, and a powerful master could keep a family safe in dangerous times. Bijapur was glad to have a soldier of his skill. And it sent him where it wanted him: far to the south, into the Karnataka country, hundreds of miles away, to win and govern lands there. A distant city, Bengaluru, slowly became his home.

He could not take a six-year-old and his wife into a life like that — years of campaigning across the far south, in country none of them knew. But he held one thing he could give them, and he gave it.

Among Shahaji's lands was Pune: a town in the hills, with villages and fields around it — a jagir, an estate granted to a noble to support him and his soldiers. The wars had knocked it about badly. But it was his to give, and he placed it in the hands of the two people he trusted most. One was Jijabai. The other was a careful, exacting, scrupulously honest administrator named Dadoji Konddev, who had served the family for years and could be counted on to manage land and money and men without stealing a coin or bending a rule.

If Shivaji asked his mother why his father would not simply come and live with them — and a six-year-old surely asked it, in one form or another — the answer would have been the one Jijabai believed to her bones.

A man's duty, she would have told him, does not always sit where his heart does. His father had lost his war; a smaller man would have sulked, or hidden, or grabbed at whatever was nearest. Shahaji had taken up a hard service instead, far from everyone he loved, so that his wife and son would be safe and provided for.

"That is not your father leaving us, Shiva," is the kind of thing she said, and meant. "That is your father carrying us — from a long way off, where you cannot see him doing it."

Whether or not those were the exact words, that was the lesson. And it sank in slowly, the way the important lessons did with him — not understood all at once, but stored, and turned over, and understood more each year.

And so, in 1636, the long safe years on Shivneri came to an end.

Six-year-old Shivaji and his mother left the great grey rock where he had been born and set out for Pune — a half-ruined town that was, somehow, now theirs to look after. His father rode the other way, south, into the rest of his life.

They would meet again in years to come. But Shivaji would never really live with Shahaji, never be raised by his hand. He grew up instead the way this story has already shown him growing — on his mother's lessons and her stories, on the memory of a burning village, on a sense of fairness that was hardening in him like a wall going up one stone at a time. From a distance, his father gave him three things: a famous name, a ruined town to bring back to life, and an example he would not fully understand for years — that doing your duty often means doing the hard, unglamorous thing, far from any praise, simply because it is yours to do.

The town was waiting. It would take all the stubbornness his mother had, and all the stubbornness he was growing, to wake it up.

What We Know

True: In 1636 the Nizamshahi kingdom of Ahmadnagar finally collapsed under Mughal pressure, ending Shahaji's long campaign to keep it alive (he really had propped up a boy-claimant on its throne). Shahaji submitted and entered the service of the Sultanate of Bijapur, which sent him to campaign and govern far south in the Karnataka; Bengaluru became his main base. He kept his Pune estate (jagir) and placed it under Jijabai and the administrator Dadoji Konddev, and it was around now that Jijabai and young Shivaji settled at Pune. Shivaji therefore grew up largely apart from his father. All of this is well documented.

Story: We have no record of a personal farewell or of anyone's exact words, so this chapter sticks to the documented shape of events and does not invent a meeting. The few lines given to Jijabai are a representative picture of her well-attested outlook on duty — not a recorded quote.

Marathi words in this chapter: jagir (an estate granted to support a noble, in return for service).
