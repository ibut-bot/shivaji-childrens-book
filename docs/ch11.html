Chapter 11 — Torna

The mountain was a black wall against the stars, and they were going to climb it in the dark, and Shivaji had never in his life been so awake.

There were perhaps thirty of them on the slope below Torna that night — Tanaji, Yesaji, a knot of the toughest hill boys, every one of them barefoot or near it, every one of them carrying a sword he had probably never yet used in earnest. Nobody spoke. They had agreed there would be no talking once they left the trees, and so the only sounds were the wind, and the small scrape of hands and feet finding the rock, and Shivaji's own heart, which seemed to him loud enough to wake the garrison all by itself.

He had chosen the hard way up on purpose.

"They watch the gate road," he had told them, the evening before, crouched over a scratch-map in the dirt. "Of course they do. It's the only sensible way up a mountain. So we don't use it." He had drawn his finger along a different line — a faint, broken seam up the fort's steep northern shoulder, a route only a goatherd or a madman would try. "We go up here. Where nobody watches, because nobody believes a person would come this way."

"Because nobody sane would," Tanaji had said.

"Which is exactly why it'll work." Shivaji had looked up at him and grinned. "Cheer up. If we fall, we won't have to worry about the part where we're hopelessly outnumbered at the top."

It had got a laugh, low and nervous, going round the fire. It was less funny now, halfway up a cliff in the cold, with the valley floor a very long way down and getting longer.

But the hill boys could climb. This was their country, vertical and unforgiving, and they went up it the way city men walk down a lane — finding holds by feel, passing a hand back to steady the next man, moving in the dark like water finding its way downhill, only upward. The thing the great armies could never understand about the Mavals was happening right here on the rock face: men who belonged to a mountain could do things on it that no trained soldier from the flat lands ever could.

Shivaji pulled himself over the last lip of rock, lay flat on the cold stone of Torna's shoulder, and looked up at the wall.

It was even sleepier than he'd hoped.

One watch-fire burned, far over by the main gate, where the guards expected trouble to come from if it came at all. Up here on the dark side there was nothing but the wall, and the grass growing thick along the top of it, and one bored sentry he could just make out, leaning on his spear, looking the wrong way — out at the empty night, not down at the impossible slope where thirty boys were now crouched in the dark like a held breath.

This, Shivaji thought, was the moment everything turned on. Not the swords. This. The fact that nobody up here truly believed an attack could happen, and so, in the way that the wall watched, the way the wall was watched, it already half hadn't.

He touched Tanaji's arm, and pointed. Tanaji nodded.

The first men went up and over the low wall like shadows pouring uphill. Shivaji counted them across in his head — five, eight, eleven — and was halfway over himself, one leg on the cold stone, when a loose lump of rock turned under a boy's foot somewhere to his left and went bouncing down the inside of the wall with a clatter that, in that held silence, sounded like the whole mountain falling.

Every one of them froze.

The sentry straightened. Turned. Took one slow step toward the sound, peering into the dark, his spear coming up — and Shivaji, flat against the stone with his heart slamming, understood that the next breath would decide everything: a single shout from that one bored man and the watch-fire would empty and the easy fort would turn, in an instant, into a trap with thirty boys caught inside it.

The sentry took another step. Squinted. Saw, perhaps, a shape that should not have been there.

He never got the shout out. Yesaji was already behind him — had gone over first, had been moving the moment the stone fell — and a hand closed over the man's mouth and an arm over his arms, and the spear was lifted gently away, and it was done, in silence, without a drop of blood. Yesaji looked back over the disarmed sentry's shoulder and found Shivaji's eye, and gave the smallest nod in the history of the world.

Shivaji let his breath out, and went the rest of the way over the wall.

What happened after that was the strangest battle Shivaji would ever fight, because it was barely a battle at all.

He had braced himself for blood. He had lain awake nights braced for it — for the clash and the screaming and the friends who might not climb back down. But a fort is not its stones; a fort is the will of the men inside it, and the few dozen men inside Torna had no will to spend dying for a mountain their distant masters had plainly forgotten. They woke to find armed strangers already inside the walls, already between them and the gate, already everywhere — quiet, fast hill-men who seemed to have grown out of the rock itself — and the heart simply went out of them.

A handful reached for weapons. Yesaji and the others were faster, and firmer, and did what had to be done with the flats of their blades and the weight of their numbers, and Shivaji's one clear order, hissed over and over in the dark, held: "Don't kill them if they'll yield. We don't want them dead. We want them gone."

And then, sooner than seemed possible, it was over. The little garrison stood huddled and disarmed in the grey before dawn, blinking at the boy who had taken their fort, and the boy was looking not at them but up — up at the bare flagpole over the gate, where the faded colours of Bijapur hung limp in the last of the night.

"Take it down," he said quietly.

They climbed the rest of the way to the high point of the fort as the sun came up.

Shivaji stood on the summit of Torna — his summit now, the highest fort in all that country — and watched the light come over the edge of the world and pour down across the ranges, hill after hill after blue-grey hill, all the way to the rim of the sky. The wind up here never stopped. It tugged at his turban and snapped the new banner above the gate, the plain bright cloth they had brought up wrapped around someone's waist and run up the empty pole with hands that wouldn't quite hold steady.

His own flag. On his own fort. Won without burying a single one of his boys.

Tanaji came and stood beside him, breathing hard, his grin so wide it looked like it hurt. "We did it," he said, as if he needed to say it out loud to believe it. "We actually—" He shook his head. "You realise no one's going to believe us."

"They'll believe the flag," Shivaji said.

Yesaji had come up on the other side, quiet as ever, looking not at the view but at Shivaji, the way he did. "They'll see the flag," Yesaji said, "from a very long way off." There was a question folded inside it, the way there always was with Yesaji.

Shivaji knew what the question was. He had been carrying it up the mountain in his chest the whole climb, heavier than any sword.

Here was the truth nobody says in the stories, the truth that arrived the instant the cheering stopped:

Taking a thing is the easy half.

For one shining hour it was pure joy — they were sixteen and seventeen and they had just done the impossible, and they laughed and thumped each other and walked the walls of their fort like kings. But Shivaji, even in the middle of it, felt the cold edge of the other thing sliding in underneath the joy. A fort you have taken is a fort you must now hold. Bijapur had forgotten Torna. Bijapur would not stay forgetful for long. Somewhere south, in a marble hall, a clerk would soon write a line in a ledger — the boy Shivaji has seized the fort of Torna — and the laughter would stop, and they would send men. Many men. Armoured men, on the kind of horses that win on open ground.

And his flag would be the thing those men rode toward.

He looked along the top of the wall, at the grass grown thick in the cracks, at the gate that sagged on its hinges, at the bastions crumbling slowly back into the mountain. The fort that had been so easy to walk into was, for that exact reason, going to be very hard to keep anyone else from walking into. It needed walls. It needed water stored, and grain, and watchmen who actually watched. It needed money — a great deal of money — and he did not have a single coin of it.

He had wanted ground to stand on. Well. Here it was, under his feet, the real and stubborn first piece of Swarajya — a vow turned into a mountain. Now he had to make it strong enough to stand the storm he had just, with his own two hands, invited.

"Yes," he said at last, answering the question Yesaji hadn't quite asked. "Now comes the hard part."

He did not sound afraid. If anything — and Tanaji, watching him, would swear to it for years afterward — he sounded glad.

The sun was fully up now. Below them, far down at the foot of the mountain, the little village was waking, and a thin child carrying a pot of water stopped in the lane, and looked up, and saw a strange new flag flying over the fort that had stood over her whole life — and stood for a long moment, just looking.

What We Know

True: It is solid, well-documented history that Torna was the very first fort Shivaji captured, around 1646, when he was about sixteen — the opening victory of his entire career, taken from the Bijapur (Adilshahi) sultanate. It is also well attested that Bijapur's grip on the fort was weak and that Shivaji took it with little or no fighting — this was not a great bloody siege but a swift, daring seizure of a neglected stronghold. Holding and rebuilding it afterward (and the money that required) genuinely became his next problem — which is the next part of the story.

Story: We have no detailed eyewitness account of how the fort was taken — the night climb up the unwatched side, the specific moment-by-moment of getting over the wall, and the dialogue here are a representative dramatisation, true to the kind of swift, low-bloodshed capture the records describe and to the mountain-craft of his Maval men. The detail that he deliberately spared the surrendering garrison fits both his documented character and his later, repeated practice of taking forts without needless killing.

Words in this chapter: Swarajya (self-rule; one's own kingdom — first sworn at Raireshwar).
