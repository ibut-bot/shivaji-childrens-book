Chhatrapati Shivaji · Volume 1 · The Boy of Shivneri

Chapter 7 — The Boys of the Mavals

Who & Where in this chapter

Characters

The guarding game had grown up, and so had the boy who invented it.

By the time he was in his teens, Shivaji was rarely at the Lal Mahal. He was out in the Maval — the green tangle of hills and valleys west of Pune — for days at a stretch, on a tough hill pony, with the dust of the high tracks on him and a widening circle of companions around him who were not servants, and not quite soldiers, and very much more than friends.

They were the boys of those hills. Farmers' sons and herders' sons, lean and barefoot and afraid of almost nothing, raised on millet and steep country. They could run up a slope that would leave a city horseman gasping. They knew every goat track, every hidden spring, every notch in the ridgeline where a person could slip from one valley to the next and vanish. The great courts of the Deccan thought of the Mavals, if they thought of them at all, as nobody — poor hill people, beneath notice.

Shivaji did not think of them as nobody. He thought of them as the most valuable people in the whole country, and he set out, without ever quite announcing it, to win their hearts.

It was not hard, in the end, because he did the one thing the great lords never did: he treated them as equals.

He ate what they ate, sitting on the ground in their villages. He learned the names of their parents and their grievances and their dogs. When they wrestled, he wrestled; when they were wrong, he told them, and when he was wrong, remarkably, he said so. He did not care which caste a boy was born into or which village he came from. He cared whether the boy was brave, and loyal, and good — and a hill that has been overlooked its whole life will give everything it has to the first great person who looks at it and sees worth.

Three of them, in time, would become names the whole country learned.

There was Tanaji Malusare, built like the hills themselves — broad, powerful, with a grin as wide as a valley and a temper that flared and forgave in the same breath. Tanaji said exactly what he thought, immediately, to anyone, and would have walked into fire backwards for Shivaji without being asked twice.

There was Yesaji Kank, quicker and quieter, a born fighter who could do things with a sword that made the others stop and stare, and who watched everything from under level brows and missed nothing.

And there was Baji Pasalkar, older than the rest — a respected deshmukh, a leader of his own valley, with grey already coming into his moustache. When a settled, senior man like Baji looked at this intense teenaged boy and decided to follow him, the younger ones noticed. It told them that whatever they were beginning to feel about Shivaji was not just a boy's daydream.

We do not know the exact day each of them threw in his lot with Shivaji, or the words that passed. But we know that they did, and we know why it held: because he gave them something nobody had ever offered hill boys before. He gave them the idea that they mattered.

What were they, exactly, this band of his?

Not an army — not yet. They had few proper weapons and no standing at all. To the sultan's officials in their distant cities, they were a pack of country youths playing at importance, not worth a second thought.

But anyone who looked closely would have seen something the officials missed. These boys knew the hill country the way no outside army ever could. They were bound to their young leader not by pay — there was none — but by something far stronger and far harder to defeat: loyalty, freely given, and a feeling that was beginning to take a shape none of them yet had a name for.

Around the cooking fires at night, high in the hills, the talk was changing. It started, as it always had, with the old complaints — the taxes that vanished into a faraway treasury, the soldiers who burned a village and rode off unpunished, the great kings who slept, as the killedar of Shivneri had said long ago, in other people's fields. But now, increasingly, the talk did not stop at complaining.

"Whose hills are these, really?" Shivaji would ask, in the kind of conversation that was happening more and more. Not the sultan's; the sultan had never set foot here and never would. "Who actually knows every path on them, defends every village, belongs to them?"

And a hill boy would poke the fire and answer, half joking, the way you say a thing you are not yet brave enough to say seriously: "We do."

"Yes," Shivaji would say, not joking at all. "We do."

He was building something, though he had not yet said the whole of it out loud — not to them, perhaps not even fully to himself.

The pieces were coming together: a mother's lessons about what a kingdom was for; an administrator's training in how a country is actually run; a sense of fairness forged watching a village burn; and now, this — a brotherhood of fierce, loyal hill people who knew their own country in their bones and had just begun to suspect that it might, one day, belong to them.

It was a dangerous set of pieces to be assembling, for the son of a man sworn to the service of Bijapur. Powerful people did not like the sound of country boys deciding whose hills these really were. And before Shivaji took the next, irreversible step, his father would see to it that he got a long, hard look at the world of power he was thinking of defying — at the glittering court of the sultan himself.

But that is the next part of the story.

What We Know

True: A core fact of Shivaji's rise is that, as a young man, he built a devoted following among the Maval people — the hardy farmers and herders of the hill-country west of Pune — and that this band of companions, intimately familiar with the terrain and fiercely loyal to him, became the foundation of his whole movement. Tanaji Malusare, Yesaji Kank, and Baji Pasalkar were all real, important companions of his early years (Baji Pasalkar a senior deshmukh among the first to support him; Tanaji a lifelong friend and famous captain). It is well remembered that Shivaji won this loyalty by treating ordinary hill people with a respect and equality that the grandees of the day did not.

Story: We don't have records of exactly how or when Shivaji first met each companion, or of specific fireside conversations, so those scenes are drawn here as a representative picture of a well-attested friendship and a documented bond — not as particular events, and the dialogue is illustrative rather than quoted.

Marathi words in this chapter: deshmukh (a hereditary local chief or district leader).
