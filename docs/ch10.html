Chapter 10 — He Who Holds the Forts

Who & Where in this chapter

Places

They lay flat on their bellies on the cold ridge, two boys pretending to be two rocks, and watched the fort do nothing at all.

It had been doing nothing for a long time. That was the interesting part.

"Forty-one," Shivaji whispered.

"You counted that one already," Tanaji whispered back. "The fat one by the gate. He's been to the wall twice."

"Then forty. I counted the goat too." Shivaji did not take his eyes off the dark shape on the mountain across the valley. "Count them again at the change of the watch. If nobody comes to take over from the men up there, that tells us something."

Tanaji shifted, trying to find a part of the ridge that wasn't made entirely of edges. "It tells me my supper's getting cold three valleys away, is what it tells me. We've been here since the sun was high."

"Good," said Shivaji. "Then you've seen what I've seen."

What they had seen was Torna.

It stood across the valley from them, the highest fort in all that country — a vast tilted mountain of grey rock with a crown of broken wall along its top, so tall that clouds caught on it in the wet season and tore. The old people called it Prachandagad, which meant something like the huge, fierce fort, and from far off it looked the part: a giant, scowling thing, impossible.

But Shivaji had not spent the whole day looking at how grand it was. He had spent the whole day looking at how tired it was.

The great gate sagged. Grass grew thick along the top of the wall, which meant no one had walked it in months. The flag of Bijapur hung from a pole, faded almost white by the sun, and below it a garrison that should have been two hundred strong was — by Shivaji's patient, all-day count — perhaps forty bored men, and not one of them had looked outward at the hills the entire time.

"They're not watching," Shivaji said, almost to himself. "A fort like this — they think the mountain does the watching for them. They think nobody would dare."

"And would they?" Tanaji asked. "Dare?"

Shivaji finally turned his head and looked at his friend, and in the last of the light Tanaji saw the particular small smile that, over the years to come, a great many powerful men would learn to dread.

"Let's go home," Shivaji said. "I've seen enough. I need to draw it."

He drew it that night by the fire, on a flat slab of stone with a piece of charcoal, and the others crowded round to watch the way they always did when he got that look.

It was not a picture of the fort. It was a picture of the whole country — the way a bird might see it, or a god. He marked the rivers as wriggling lines and the roads as straight ones and the towns as little squares. And then, all across it, he began to mark the forts: Torna, here; and Rajgad's hill, there; and Kondhana, and Purandar, and a dozen more, each one a hard grey dot sitting on its own mountain, watching over the valleys and the roads below.

"Look," he said. "What do you see?"

The boys looked. Tanaji saw a lot of dots and a lot of squiggles and said so.

"Look again." Shivaji laid his charcoal on a road, and traced it. "Here's a road. Grain goes along it. Taxes go along it. The sultan's soldiers go along it. Now — who decides what passes?" He tapped the grey dot sitting above the road. "Not the man on the road. The man on the fort. He can see for thirty miles. He can come down, or stay up where no one can reach him. Whoever holds the high stone holds everything underneath it."

Yesaji, who said little and missed nothing, leaned in. "And right now," he said slowly, "who holds them?"

"Men three valleys away who've forgotten they own them," said Shivaji. "Men who think a fort guards itself."

There was a thing he had to make them understand, and he knew it, because what he was thinking of doing was the kind of idea that gets boys killed if only half of them believe it.

"We can't fight them in the open," he said. "I want you to hear me say it plainly, because some day one of you is going to be brave and stupid and want to. We can't. They have cavalry — thousands of armoured horsemen on the flat ground, where horses win. We have a few dozen lads with country swords and very good legs. On open ground they would ride us down before breakfast and not remember it by lunch."

He let that sit. Nobody argued. They had all seen the sultanate cavalry — the lobed steel plate, the lances, the heavy horses. You did not have to be told twice.

"So we don't fight them where they're strong," Shivaji went on. "We fight them where we're strong. Up there." He pointed his chin at the dark hills all around them. "Where a horse is useless and a goat track is a highway and one man who knows the ground is worth a hundred who don't. We don't beat their army. We just" — he spread his hand flat over his charcoal map, over all the little grey dots — "quietly take the stones they stopped watching. One. Then the next. Then the next."

"A fort at a time," said Tanaji, and for once he was not joking.

"A fort at a time," Shivaji agreed. "Each one we hold is a place the people around it can run to. A place we can see the roads from. A place they can't get us out of without paying more than we're worth." He looked around the ring of firelit faces. "An army you can beat in one bad afternoon. But a country full of forts, each one held by men who'd die before they gave it up — that you have to win a hundred times over. And we live here. We can lose a hundred times and still be here. Can they?"

He was quiet for a moment, and when he spoke again it was lower, and it was not about strategy at all.

"There's a village at the foot of Torna," he said. "I watched it all day too, while I was counting bored soldiers. Children carrying water. A man mending a wall. They've had that grey giant standing over them their whole lives, and not once has it kept the raiders out of their fields — because the men inside it work for someone far away who has never thought about them for a single heartbeat." He looked at the fire. "Imagine a fort that did. That looked down and saw its own people. That came when a village called." He shook his head slowly. "That's the difference. Not whose flag is on the pole. Whose roof it is."

Nobody answered. They had all, one way or another, grown up under a sky that turned to fire and a fort that did nothing.

It was, though none of them had the word for it that night, the whole of his life's strategy, worked out by a boy of sixteen on a slab of rock by a cooking fire. He would never really change it. Thirty years of war, against the richest empires in the world, would all come back to this: don't meet the storm in the open field. Make yourself a thousand forts. Make the land itself fight on your side.

"So," said Yesaji, in the quiet after, watching him the way he watched everything. "Which one?"

Shivaji did not have to ask which one which one meant. He looked down at his map — at all the grey dots scattered across the dark stone — and he put one finger, gently, on the tallest, tiredest, most forgotten of them all.

"This one," he said. "The big fierce one that nobody's watching. We take the proudest fort in the country while its owners are asleep, and we do it without losing a single boy if we can manage it." The small smile again. "And then we see how loudly Bijapur can pretend it didn't happen."

Tanaji laughed out loud — he couldn't help it — and clapped a hand over his own mouth, three valleys from anyone, as if the sleeping fort might hear.

But Shivaji wasn't laughing now. He was looking at the dot under his finger, and past it, at the thing the dot was really for. A vow needed ground to stand on. He had said so in the cold temple at Raireshwar, with the blood barely dry on the stone. Here, then, was the ground. The first plain, stubborn, real piece of it.

All they had to do was climb a mountain in the dark and take it.

How hard could that be?

What We Know

True: It is solid history that Torna (also called Prachandagad) was the first fort Shivaji took, around 1646, when he was about sixteen — the opening move of his entire career. And the strategy this chapter describes is absolutely real and absolutely his: rather than meet the great sultanate and Mughal armies in open battle, where their heavy cavalry was overwhelming, Shivaji built his power on hill forts and on fighting in the mountains his Maval men knew intimately. Controlling the forts meant controlling the roads, the revenue, and the countryside. This fort-based, terrain-based warfare became the foundation of the Maratha state and is studied to this day.

Story: We have no eyewitness account of Shivaji personally scouting Torna from a ridge, or of a particular fireside where he sketched a map and explained his plan — so that scene, and the dialogue in it, is drawn here as a representative picture of a strategy that is very well documented. The neglected, lightly-held state of the fort under a distant Bijapur is consistent with how he was able to take it; the exact numbers are illustrative.

Words in this chapter: Prachandagad (Torna's other name — roughly "the huge, fierce fort").
