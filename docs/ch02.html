Chapter 2 — A Boy Among Soldiers

The army on the plain had a thousand fires, and Shivaji meant to count every one.

He lay on his stomach on the cold stone of the rampart, chin on his folded arms, lips moving in the dark. "...nine, ten, eleven..." Far below, the fires blinked across the black plain like a second sky that had come loose and fallen to the ground.

"You'll be at it till the sun comes up," said the killedar.

The old man stood behind him, a shadow against the stars, exactly where he had stood on a winter dawn five years before — though the boy did not know that, and the killedar did not say it. He only watched the small back, the kicking heels, the fierce concentration, and felt something in his chest he could not have named.

"Whose are they?" Shivaji asked, without looking round. "The fires."

"By night, no one's. Fires all look the same in the dark." The killedar lowered himself down beside him with a grunt. "By daylight you can read them. They carry their flags."

"Read them how?"

"Colours. Shapes. A man learns." He pointed a knotted finger out into the dark. "That lot came up from the south two days ago. Tomorrow they'll be gone north, and the day after, someone else will come up the same road going the other way."

Shivaji finally turned his head. He was five years old, all black hair and elbows, with eyes that took a thing apart and put it back together. "Why do they keep walking up and down?"

"Because this land has three masters," the old man said, "and obeys none of them."

By daylight, he showed the boy what he meant.

In one season they watched three armies cross the country below Shivneri. The first came under green banners — the Sultan of Bijapur's men, riding south. The second flew the colours of Ahmadnagar, what was left of it, a kingdom being squeezed to death between bigger ones. And late in the year came the third and largest, an ocean of horsemen and elephants and dust under the flags of the Mughal Emperor, who ruled half the world and wanted the other half.

"Three kings," Shivaji said, frowning down at them. "Where do they all sleep?"

"In other people's fields," said the killedar.

That was the part the boy could not stop looking at. Not the soldiers — the fields. Wherever an army passed, it left the land behind it flattened and black, the villages emptied, the wells fouled. And then there was no rain, two years running, and what the armies had not trampled simply withered in the ground.

The grain ran out.

It ran out in the villages first, and then it began to run low even on the fort, where the great stone storehouses had always seemed bottomless. Shivaji noticed it the way children notice things — not in numbers, but in his stomach. The bhakri came smaller. The cook's face got tighter. And down at the foot of the hill, where the road wound up toward the first gate, the people began to gather.

They came a few at a time, and then in their dozens, and then there were too many to count, which for Shivaji was the most frightening number of all.

He stood at the parapet above the gate and looked down at them — whole families sitting in the dust with their bundles, looking up at the walls. Mothers with babies. Old men. Children his own size, only thinner, with the still, patient faces of children who had stopped expecting things.

"Why don't they come in?" he asked.

Jijabai had come up quietly beside him. She did not answer at once. She was looking down at the people with an expression he would spend the rest of his life trying to deserve.

"Because a fort cannot hold everyone," she said at last. "And because the men who command forts do not always remember that the people down there are the reason a fort is worth holding at all."

"We have grain."

"Less than we did."

"But we have some." He looked up at her, and there was no cleverness in it, only the plain arithmetic of a child. "They have none. We have some. So."

Jijabai looked at her son for a long moment. Then, to the astonishment of the cook and the worry of the killedar, she had the gates opened — not wide, never wide, but enough. Enough for the grain to go out. Pots of it, basket after basket, carried down the winding road by the fort's own servants and shared among the people in the dust, while the soldiers watched from the walls and said nothing, because the lady of the fort was standing among them with her chin up, daring anyone to call it foolish.

Shivaji went down with the baskets. No one told him to. He simply went, clutching his own piece of bhakri, and when he reached the bottom he found himself standing in front of a boy about his own age, with a hollow face and enormous eyes, and he did not know what to say, so he said nothing and held out the bread.

The other boy took it in both hands. He did not gobble it. He broke it in half and gave one half to the smaller child beside him, and only then began to eat.

Shivaji watched this, and something happened in him that did not have words yet, but would have them later. It felt like anger and love at the same time. It felt like a promise he was too young to make.

Once that winter a rider came who was not an enemy.

He was thin, as everyone was thin now, and his horse was thinner, and he had ridden through country that swallowed men whole. But in his saddlebag was a folded square of paper with a seal Jijabai knew, and her hands went very still as she opened it.

Shivaji knew the ritual by now. A letter from his father came at the full moon, more or less, when it came at all. He had never seen his father. Shahaji was a word, a seal, a grey horse in a story — a man fighting somewhere out beyond the fires, for one of those kings who slept in other people's fields.

"What does he say?"

"That he is alive. That the war goes badly. That he is still ours." She folded the paper along its old creases and laid it in the small brass box with the others, where a little stack of them had grown year by year. "He always says the same three things."

"Read me the part with my name in it."

She found it — kiss the boy for me — and read it out, and Shivaji listened with his whole body, trying to hear a voice he had never heard, in words set down by a hand he had never held. Then he went back to the wall to count soldiers, because counting soldiers was a thing he knew how to do, and missing a father he had never met was not.

The danger came three days afterward, and it came on horseback.

A band of riders broke off from one of the passing armies — perhaps forty of them, lean and hungry as everyone was hungry now — and came up the road expecting an easy meal. They wore the steel and silk of a Deccan sultanate, conical helmets and chain mail under bright coats, and curved swords that had clearly been used. Their captain rode to within shouting distance of the first gate and called up that the fort would open its stores to His Majesty's loyal soldiers, and be quick about it.

Shivaji was lifted off the wall and carried inside despite his furious wriggling. But he heard the killedar's answer ring off the stone, calm as a closing door.

"His Majesty's loyal soldiers," the old man called down, "are welcome to climb up and collect it. There are seven gates. They may start whenever they like."

Nobody started. The riders sat in the road a long while, looking up at the angled walls and the spiked doors and the unhurried men along the parapet, doing the same arithmetic the boy had done, only backwards. Then they wheeled their horses and rode away to find somewhere softer, and the fort breathed again.

That evening Shivaji marched up and down the courtyard with a wooden sword the carpenter had cut for him, scowling tremendously, defending the gate against forty invisible horsemen and defeating them all.

Jijabai watched him from the doorway and did not laugh, though her mouth wanted to. She was thinking that the sword was a toy, and the gate was real, and that the distance between the two was exactly the size of a childhood — and that childhoods, in this country, in these years, did not last very long.

"Enough soldiering," she called. "Come inside. It's cold."

"One more. There's one more left."

"There is always one more left," she said. "That is the trouble with armies. Inside, soldier."

He came, dragging the wooden sword, and she wrapped him in a shawl by the small flame of the lamp, and he leaned his black head against her and was, all at once, only five years old after all.

"The boy at the gate," he murmured, already half asleep. "He gave half away. Even though he was hungry."

"I saw."

"Why?"

Jijabai was quiet for a moment, and the lamp threw the shapes of old stories onto the wall — kings and demons and a bow that only the true could bend.

"Let me tell you about a prince," she said softly, "who gave away a whole kingdom, and was greater for it than any king who ever kept one."

And outside, on the plain, the fires of somebody's army burned down to embers — but inside the oldest, strongest fort in the Maval hills, a boy fell asleep listening to the first of the stories that would tell him, all his life, what a king was truly for.

What We Know

True: Shivaji's earliest years really did fall in one of the worst stretches the Deccan ever knew. Between about 1630 and 1632 a catastrophic famine — caused by failed rains and made far worse by constant warfare — killed enormous numbers of people across the region. Three powers really were fighting back and forth across this land: the Sultanate of Bijapur (Adilshahi), the dying Sultanate of Ahmadnagar (Nizamshahi), and the Mughal Empire pressing down from the north. Ordinary farming families suffered the most, exactly as shown.

Story: The specific scenes — counting the fires, the grain carried down to the refugees, the boy at the gate, the band of riders turned away — are invented to let us see those years through a child's eyes. We don't have a diary of Shivaji's fifth winter. But growing up surrounded by this suffering, in his mother's care, is very much part of the real story of how he came to believe that protecting ordinary people was a ruler's first duty.

Marathi words in this chapter: bhakri (a flat round bread of millet, everyday food in the Deccan).
