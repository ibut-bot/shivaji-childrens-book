Chapter 14 — Farewell, Teacher

Who & Where in this chapter

The man who had checked every figure in every ledger of the Pune lands for fifteen years could no longer hold the pen.

Shivaji noticed it before Dadoji admitted it — the way the old man's hand trembled over the column of numbers, set the pen down, and pretended he had only paused to think. Dadoji Konddev did not complain. He had never in his life complained about anything except sloppy arithmetic and unfair taxes. But he was old now, properly old, grey gone to white, and the winter had got into his chest and would not leave, and everyone in the Lal Mahal had begun to speak more softly in the passages, the way people do when they know.

"Read me the western villages' accounts," Dadoji told him, that last week, propped up among cushions because he could no longer sit at the desk. "And do not skip the small entries because you think I'm too tired to check them. I will know."

"I know you'll know," Shivaji said, and read him the accounts, every small entry, while the old man listened with his eyes closed and corrected him twice.

They had spent a great deal of Shivaji's life disagreeing, the two of them, and they both knew it, and it had never once touched the bone-deep respect underneath.

Dadoji was a careful man. He believed in ledgers that balanced and walls that held and bills that were paid and, above all, in not poking the lion. He had served the great powers of the Deccan his whole life because that was how a sensible person kept his lands and his people safe, and he had watched his brilliant, beloved, impossible student spend the last few years doing the precise opposite — taking the lion's forts, writing the lion sweet lying letters, building a kingdom the lion had never said he could have.

"You frighten me, you know," the old man said one evening, out of a long silence. His voice was thin now, but the dry edge was still in it. "You always did. Even as a small boy. You'd ask a question, and I'd answer it, and you'd look at me as if you were deciding whether to believe me." A faint wheeze that was almost a laugh. "No other child ever did that."

"You always answered the questions, though," Shivaji said. "Even the dangerous ones."

"Yes. That was my mistake." But he was smiling, barely. "I taught you to read a land-record and settle a village quarrel and weigh a fair tax. Safe things. Steward's things. And you took all of it" — the frail hand lifted, and turned, taking in the whole world beyond the window, the hills full of his forts — "and built that out of it. I gave you a set of tools for keeping a garden. You've used them to start a war."

"I've used them," Shivaji said quietly, "exactly the way you taught me. For the people on the land. You're the one who taught me that a tax should be fair, and a record honest, and a strong man not allowed to rob a weak one. I just decided it shouldn't stop at the edge of one jagir."

The old man was quiet for a long moment.

"Yes," he said at last, and something in him seemed to set down a weight it had carried for years. "Yes. I suppose you did."

Near the very end, when his voice had almost gone, Dadoji called the young man close, and Shivaji bent down to hear him.

"Listen to me. Taking is easy. Any bandit can take." The words came slow, one at a time, each one spent like a coin from a nearly empty purse. "Keeping is the hard thing. Keeping it well. When I am gone, and there is no dull old man to make you balance your books — you balance them anyway. Be just when no one is making you. Tax fairly when you could tax greedily. Protect the small ones when it would be cheaper not to." His clouded eyes found Shivaji's face. "Govern well. It is harder than fighting well. You know this. I taught it to you. Do not forget it the moment I stop saying it."

"I won't forget," Shivaji said. His voice was not quite steady. "I promise you. I won't forget."

Dadoji Konddev nodded, once, satisfied at last — the way he used to nod over a column of figures that finally came out right — and a little while after that, quietly, with the winter wind at the windows and his impossible student holding his hand, the old teacher died.

Shivaji wept for him. He was seventeen, and he had lost the steady, exacting, ever-present man who had stood in for an absent father since he was six years old, and he did not pretend he hadn't. Jijabai stood beside her son at the funeral with her back as straight as ever and her own grief held quiet behind her eyes, and afterwards the Lal Mahal was a different house, with a silence in it where a dry voice used to correct the accounts.

And then, because the world does not stop for grief, there was a jagir to run — and now, for the first time, no guardian to run it.

There was no Dadoji to sign the orders. So Shivaji signed them himself.

When his first order went out from the Lal Mahal under his own authority, it carried a new mark pressed into the wax — a seal he had chosen with great care, and it was not like the seals of the sultanate officials all around him.

Theirs were in Persian, the language of their distant masters. Shivaji's was in Sanskrit, the old language of this land, cut in clear Devanagari letters around an eight-sided stamp. And it did not say property of so-and-so or servant of the sultan. It said something a boy had been working toward his whole life, in words his mother and his teacher would both have known by heart without ever having read them:

Like the first sliver of the new moon, this seal of Shiva, son of Shahaji, will only grow — and it shines for the good of the people.

He pressed it into the warm wax and lifted it away, and there it was: not a servant's mark, but a ruler's — small, and quiet, and the most defiant thing he had ever done. A boy with a few hill forts and no crown and no permission from anyone alive had just told the world, in the oldest language he knew, that he intended to grow, and that everything he built would be for the people on the land.

It was exactly what Jijabai had taught him a king should be. It was exactly what Dadoji had spent his last breath asking him to be.

He looked at the small red mark a long time. Then he sent the order, and got to work.

He did not know — could not have known — how soon and how cruelly that promise would be tested. The careful old man who had spent his life warning don't poke the lion had only just been laid to rest. And far to the south, the lion was finally, fully awake. It had decided the time for sweet letters was over.

And it knew exactly where to reach to hurt the boy in the hills the most.

What We Know

True: Dadoji Konddev, the able administrator who managed the Pune jagir and oversaw the young Shivaji's upbringing and education, really did die around 1647, when Shivaji was about seventeen — after which Shivaji took full charge of the estate himself. It is also true and well documented that Shivaji used a distinctive royal seal, the Rajmudra, written in Sanskrit (in Devanagari) rather than the Persian used by the sultanates — and its words really do declare that, like the ever-waxing new moon, his rule would grow and would exist for the welfare of the people. That seal is one of the clearest windows we have into what he was actually trying to do.

Story: The exact words and scenes at Dadoji's deathbed are an imagined, representative dramatisation — we don't have a record of his final conversations — though they reflect the genuine relationship (a careful, loyal steward guiding a far bolder student) and Dadoji's documented role as teacher of both soldiering and fair administration. The precise moment Shivaji first adopted his Sanskrit seal is uncertain; we've placed it here, as he steps fully into authority, because that is what it truly represents. (Dadoji is remembered in different ways by different people; we've kept to his clear role as Shivaji's teacher and the estate's steward.)

Words in this chapter: Rajmudra (Shivaji's royal seal), Devanagari (the script used to write Sanskrit and Marathi).
