Chapter 15 — The Trap

Who & Where in this chapter

Characters

The messenger had ridden so hard that he could barely stand, and when he finally got the words out, the whole room went very still.

"They've taken your father. Shahaji. Arrested — in the south, on the Sultan's orders. Chained, and carried to Bijapur."

For a moment Shivaji did not move at all. Then he said, very quietly, "How."

"Treachery, lord. Another Maratha — Baji Ghorpade — a sardar in Bijapur's pay. He went to your father as a friend, under a flag of friendship, and then he sprang the trap." The messenger swallowed. "They put him in chains like a common thief. And, lord — they are saying that unless certain things happen, unless certain forts are handed over and a certain son sits down and stops causing trouble..." He could not finish it.

"Say it," said Jijabai.

The messenger looked at the floor. "They are threatening to put him to death."

So there it was. The lion had found the one way to reach him that no wall could stop.

Shivaji had spent years making himself untouchable — hidden forts, loyal hills, a kingdom of stone the sultan's heavy horse could never climb. And Bijapur, unable to catch the son, had simply reached out and seized the father instead. It was the cruellest, cleverest move on the whole board, and he had never seen it coming, and the worst part — the part that made him feel sick — was that it was his own doing that had put the noose around Shahaji's neck. He had taken the forts. He had flown his flags. And his father, far away in the south, faithfully serving the very masters his son defied, was going to pay for it.

He thought of the man he barely knew. The voice he had only ever heard read aloud from folded letters. Kiss the boy for me. The father he had met properly only a handful of times, who had once warned him, in a hall of gold, that a noble must sometimes bow — that is the price of doing any good in a world owned by other men.

This was the price. And it had come due all at once.

He wanted to ride south that very night. Jijabai stopped him.

"And do what?" she asked. Her voice was level, the way it was always level when everything was on fire. "Bijapur is a hundred leagues away, behind an army, behind walls. You cannot punch your way to your father. If you ride south in a rage, you will die on the road, and then he will die in his cell, and everything the two of you have built will die with you. That is exactly what they want. A grieving boy does foolish things."

"So I do nothing?" The word came out raw.

"You do the right thing, which is harder." She took his face in her hands, this mother who did not flinch where others could see. "Your father is a soldier. He knew this could come. He would not want you to throw away the work of his whole life — and yours — to charge a wall and feel better for one brave minute. Be cleverer than your grief, Shivaji. It is the only weapon that will actually reach him."

He stood there breathing hard, the boy who took forts by talking, the boy who had sworn never to be a storm — and he understood that the hardest battle of his life so far was going to be fought standing still.

But he was not to be given even that much time.

Because Bijapur, having trapped the father, now moved to crush the son.

While Shahaji sat in chains, the Sultan sent an army north into the hills to finish the troublesome boy once and for all — a real army, led by a real general, a hard and capable commander named Fateh Khan who had no intention of sitting down to tea. This was no sleepy fort-keeper to be talked around. This was exactly the thing Shivaji had warned his friends about, that night by the fire after the easy victories: one day they'll send someone who isn't tired, and isn't reasonable, and can't be talked around.

That day had come. And it had come at the worst possible moment, with his father in a cell and his own heart in pieces.

Tanaji found him on the walls of Purandar, looking south. "Fateh Khan's crossed into the hills," he said. "Thousands of them. Horse, foot, guns. Coming straight for us." He hesitated. "The men are frightened, Shivaji. We've never fought anything like this. We've taken sleepy forts and talked old men out of keys. This is different."

"I know it's different." Shivaji turned from the wall, and Tanaji was startled to see that the grief in his face had gone somewhere far down, and something colder and clearer had come up in its place. "They think this is the moment we break. Father in chains, an army at the door — they think we'll panic, or surrender, or run." He almost smiled, and it was not a nice smile. "Let's teach them what these hills do to armies that come up them too sure of themselves."

He did not meet Fateh Khan in the open, where the heavy cavalry would have ridden the Mavals into the dust. He did everything he had spent years preparing to do.

He fought where the hills fought for him — in the broken country below Purandar, around the village of Belsar, where a horse was a burden and a goat track was a road and every boulder might hide a man who knew the ground. He let the great army grind its way up into the steep places, strung out and blind and far from home, and then the hills came alive around it. The Mavals struck from where they could not be struck back, fast and everywhere and gone, the way water is everywhere and cannot be punched. Fateh Khan's soldiers, who had expected to crush a frightened boy, found themselves fighting an enemy that was somehow above them and below them and behind them and never, ever where the lance came down.

It was, in the end, a rout. The proud army that had marched up so certain marched back down in pieces, and Fateh Khan with it — beaten, badly, by a teenager and a few thousand hill-men in a tangle of rocks. It was Shivaji's first true battle, the first time he had stood against a real army in the open field of war.

He won it.

But it was not like taking Torna in the dark, or Kondhana over tea.

This time there was a cost, and he made himself walk through every inch of it when the fighting was done — past the wounded, past the still shapes the Mavals were carrying gently down off the mountain, past the places where the hills had been paid for in something far dearer than gold. Among the fallen was a face he had known for years: Baji Pasalkar, the grey old deshmukh who had been one of the very first important men to look at a teenaged dreamer and decide to follow him. He would follow no further.

Shivaji knelt by him a long time.

"This," he said at last, to Tanaji, to Yesaji, to all of them gathered quiet and stricken in the dusk, "is the other kind. I told you it would come. The kind that can't be won with a clever word." His voice was rough. "Look at it. Look properly, all of you, and never get to like it. We fight when they leave us nothing else — and they left us nothing else today. But we do not celebrate this. A man is not a fort. You don't get him back by repairing a wall."

The hills were silent around them. Far to the south, his father was still in chains, and no battle, however brave, had changed that by a single link.

That was the bitter lesson under the victory, and Shivaji took it in fully, standing on that darkening hillside.

He had beaten the army they sent. He had shown the whole Deccan that hurting his father would not make him kneel — that it made the hills more dangerous, not less. But beating Fateh Khan had not opened a single door of Shahaji's cell. His father was a prisoner of men a hundred leagues away, behind walls Shivaji could not climb and an army he could not reach, and every sword in the Sahyadris could not cut him loose.

For that, he would need a different kind of weapon entirely. Not a battle. Not even a fort.

A letter — and the nerve to send it to the most dangerous man in India.

What We Know

True: In July 1648, Shivaji's father Shahaji really was arrested on the orders of the Bijapur (Adilshahi) Sultan — seized through the treachery of a rival Maratha noble, Baji Ghorpade — and held prisoner, under real threat, partly as a way to control his increasingly troublesome son. In response, Bijapur sent an army under the general Fateh Khan to crush Shivaji, and in 1648 Shivaji decisively defeated it in the hill country around Purandar and Belsar — his first major battle and victory, which established him as a serious military power. Baji Pasalkar, one of his important early companions, is remembered as dying fighting in these early battles. Shahaji was released the following year (1649) — but that is the next part of the story.

Story: The exact conversations — the messenger, Jijabai steadying her son, Shivaji's words over the fallen — are an imagined dramatisation of documented events. Historians note the causes of Shahaji's arrest are debated: Maratha accounts blame Shivaji's rebellion, while Bijapur's own records point more to Shahaji's independent dealings far away in the Karnataka — so "they seized the father to control the son" is the traditional framing, simplified here. We've kept the battle's violence off the page, as we do throughout, showing its cost through grief rather than gore. (Note: the famous Battle of Purandar that many people know — against the Mughals — happened much later, in 1665, and belongs to a later book.)

Words in this chapter: sardar (a noble, chief, or military commander).
