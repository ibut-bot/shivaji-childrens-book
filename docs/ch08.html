Chapter 8 — The Court of Bijapur

The first time Shivaji saw the city of Bijapur, he understood that he had been living his whole life in a small, poor, rough corner of the world — and that he did not mind at all.

Bijapur was the capital of the Adilshahi sultanate, his father's masters, and it was magnificent. Great domes floated above the rooftops. The bazaars overflowed with silk and spice and silver. Soldiers in fine coats stood at gates taller than three men. After the bare brown hills of the Maval, it should have dazzled a boy completely.

It did dazzle him. But even as it did, something in him was watching it the way Dadoji had taught him to watch a ledger — looking for what it cost, and who paid.

He had come because his father had sent for him. Shahaji was a great noble of this court now, one of its most valued generals, and it was time, he had decided, for his son to see the world of power up close — to learn how it worked, and perhaps to be tempted by it.

The heart of that world was the durbar, the great audience hall, and on the day Shivaji was brought into it, it was everything a court should be.

The hall was a cavern of colour and perfume. Hundreds of nobles stood in their ranks in cloth-of-gold and jewels, each in his exact and proper place. Incense hung in the air. Somewhere a fountain played. And at the far end, raised high on a throne beneath a canopy, small with distance and blazing with gems, sat the Sultan of Bijapur himself — the master of all these glittering men.

One by one, as their names were called, the great nobles approached the throne. And one by one, each of them — these proud generals and governors, men who commanded thousands — stopped at the proper distance and bowed. Not a small bow. The full court obeisance: low, deep, hands sweeping to the floor, the whole body folded in submission before the man on the chair.

Shivaji watched his father's turn come. He watched Shahaji — the warrior on the grey horse, the man whose name soldiers spoke with respect across the whole Deccan, the tallest man Shivaji had ever thought existed — walk to the line, and stop, and bow himself low to the floor before the Sultan, like all the rest.

And something in the boy went very cold and very still.

What happened next is the story Bijapur and the Marathas have told ever since — and it is a story, remembered and retold and probably polished smooth over the centuries, so we cannot be sure of the details.

But the story goes like this. When it was the boy's turn — when young Shivaji was led forward to make his own bow before the Sultan of Bijapur — he did not make it. Or he made only the smallest, stiffest nod a person could make and still call it courtesy, and then he straightened up and looked at the man on the throne with steady, level, unfrightened eyes, as if to say: you are a man on a chair. I will be polite to you. I will not crawl for you.

The court, the story says, was scandalized. His father was alarmed. A boy could get a family ruined for far less. And whether it happened exactly so, or whether the tale grew in the telling, everyone who has ever repeated it has understood what it means — because it is the truest thing about him. Shivaji had looked at the most dazzling power in his world, and decided, in his bones, that he did not want it. Not if the price of it was that.

Afterwards — and this part, too, is how the story is told — his father took him aside, somewhere quieter, and tried to explain the way the world actually worked.

A noble bows, Shahaji would have told him. That is the bargain. You bow to the Sultan, and in return you are given lands, and rank, and the strength to protect your own people. A man who will not bend is broken, and a broken man protects no one. I bow, Shiva — do you think I enjoy it? I bow so that you and your mother are safe. That is not weakness. That is the price of doing any good at all in a world owned by other men.

And Shivaji, the story says, listened to all of it, and understood it, and even loved his father more for the weariness in it.

And still did not change his mind.

Because the boy had seen something in that golden hall that the grown men in it had stopped being able to see. He had seen that all the silk and all the domes and all the jewels rested, in the end, on one simple fact: that everyone here knelt to someone else. The nobles knelt to the Sultan. The Sultan, far away, schemed and bowed in his turn to the Mughal Emperor. It was kneeling all the way up — a whole world of powerful men, none of them truly free, each one's grandeur built on bending the knee to someone bigger.

That is not a kingdom, the boy thought. That is just a very rich kind of slavery.

He went back to his hills not long after, and he was quieter than usual on the road, turning it all over.

He was not against power. He had felt its pull in that hall as strongly as anyone. But he understood now, completely, exactly what kind he wanted and what kind he refused. He did not want to be the richest, most jewelled noble in someone else's court, bowing low for the right to exist. If he ever held power, it would be a different thing entirely — a land where his own people knelt to no foreign throne at all. Where the ordinary farmer in the Maval and the boy at the gate with his half-loaf answered to their own, and bowed to no Sultan and no Emperor for the right to keep their own grain.

There was even a word for such a thing, an old and almost forgotten idea: Swarajya. Self-rule. One's own kingdom.

He did not say it out loud yet. But somewhere on the road home from the most glorious court in the Deccan, a boy who had refused to bow began, very seriously, to think about how a person might actually build a land worth not bowing in.

It was nearly time, now, for an oath.

What We Know

True: Bijapur, capital of the Adilshahi sultanate, was genuinely one of the wealthiest and most splendid courts of the 17th-century Deccan, famous for its architecture, art, and elaborate ceremony. Shahaji really was a great and valued noble in its service, and the etiquette of such a court really did require deep, formal acts of obeisance before the Sultan. Shivaji's lifelong refusal to accept servitude under outside powers — and his ideal of Swarajya (self-rule) — are absolutely central and well documented.

Story (legend): The famous, much-loved tale that young Shivaji was taken to the Bijapur court and there pointedly refused to bow before the Sultan is tradition, not firmly documented history — a story retold and shaped over centuries. We've told it here clearly as the legend it is. What is solid is its meaning, which matches everything we do know about him: he looked at the glittering, kneeling world of sultanate power and wanted no part of it.

Marathi & court words in this chapter: durbar (a royal court or formal audience), Swarajya (self-rule; one's own kingdom).
