# Chapter 5 — The City of Ashes

When they finally came to Pune, Shivaji's town, the boy stood up in the cart to see it — and could not find it.

He had built a Pune in his head on the long road west: his father's town, the place that was *theirs*. He had imagined walls and busy lanes and a market. What lay in front of him was a grey scatter of broken things. Burnt house-stumps. Fallen walls with grass growing out of them. Fields gone to thorn and scrub, wells choked, lanes empty. A few thin dogs. No people.

"This is it?" he said. "This is the town?"

"This is the town," said Jijabai. She did not sound dismayed. She sounded the way she did when she had decided something. "It was a fine one, once. The wars killed it. Now we bring it back."

---

There was a reason no one lived in Pune, and it was not only the wars.

The story the country told — and it is only a story, though people believed it with their whole hearts — was that when the enemy had finished smashing the town, they had *cursed* it. They had taken a plough, the tale goes, and harnessed it not to an ox but to a donkey, and dragged it across the dead ground in a long ugly furrow, and driven an iron bar into the earth, to say to anyone who might think of returning: *this place is finished forever. Let it be wasteland. Whoever rebuilds here will be cursed as it is cursed.*

True or not, the curse did its work, because people are afraid of curses. The land sat empty and feared while the thorn grew over it. To live in Pune, you would first have to not be afraid — and after these years, in this country, almost everyone was afraid of something.

Jijabai was not. Neither, it turned out, was the careful old man who came to help her.

---

His name was Dadoji Konddev, and Shahaji had not chosen him by accident.

He was an administrator to his bones — lean, grey, exacting, with a habit of looking at a field or a ledger or a person and seeing exactly what it was worth and exactly what was wrong with it. He did not believe in curses. He believed in water, and seed, and fair dealing, and the slow patient arithmetic of bringing dead ground back to life.

"A curse I cannot lift," he told Jijabai on his first survey of the ruin, dry as ever. "But a blocked well I can clear. A burnt field I can plough. Frightened farmers I can coax back, if we give them a reason to trust that this time the grain will be theirs to keep." He looked around at the desolation with no fear in his face at all, only sums. "It will take years."

"Then we had better start today," said Jijabai.

And they did. Word went out into the hills: come back to Pune. Your old land is yours again. The taxes will be fair, set down in writing, and no soldier and no strongman will rob you of your harvest while the lady of the Lal Mahal has anything to say about it. Come home.

---

To break the fear, though, the curse itself had to be answered — and here the country tells its favourite story of all.

They say that to undo the enemy's ugly furrow, Jijabai called for a plough of her own — but a plough of *gold*. They say that on a chosen day, with the few returning families watching, the dead and cursed ground of Pune was ploughed again from end to end with that golden plough, the first bright furrow turned to lift the darkness off the land and give it back to the living. A curse cut with a donkey, they say, was undone with gold and courage.

Whether it happened with real gold, or whether the gold is the way the story remembers something braver and plainer — a woman and a boy and a handful of frightened farmers deciding, together, to stop being afraid — no one can now say for certain. But that was the meaning of it, and the meaning was true. The first furrow was turned. The ground did not swallow anyone. And the watching families let out a long breath, and picked up their tools, and began.

Shivaji never forgot it. He had spent his short life so far being protected behind strong walls. Here, for the first time, he watched something *built* — and learned that to face down an enemy you do not always need a sword. Sometimes you need a plough, and a roof, and the plain stubbornness to begin.

---

The town came back the way the old man had promised: slowly, and then all at once.

The wells were dug out and ran clean. The thorn was burned off and the black fields turned green again with millet and sugarcane. Lanes filled. A market reopened. Children — real ones, not imaginary soldiers — ran in the streets. And in the middle of the reviving town there rose a new house for Jijabai and her son: the **Lal Mahal**, the "Red Mansion," with its warm red-brown walls and its courtyards and its veranda from which a boy could watch his town wake up around him a little more each day.

It was not a fort on a lonely rock. It was something Shivaji had never really had: a *home*, in the middle of his own people, in a place that he and his mother had brought back from the dead.

"You see?" Jijabai said to him one evening, the two of them watching the lamps come on across the rebuilt lanes. "They cursed it so no one would dare. And we dared. That is a thing worth remembering, Shiva — that the bravest thing a person can do is not always to fight. Sometimes it is to *build*, where everyone else was too afraid to."

The boy looked out at the lights that had not been there a year ago, in the town that was supposed to stay dead forever, and he remembered it all his life.

There was a great deal still to learn — how to ride, how to fight, how to govern, how to turn a furious sense of fairness into something that could actually protect people. And the careful old man who had raised a town from its ashes was about to start teaching him.

---

### What We Know

**True:** Pune really had been devastated in the Deccan wars before Shivaji's time. Under **Jijabai** and the administrator **Dadoji Konddev** — placed there by Shahaji — the town and its surrounding lands were deliberately **rebuilt and resettled** from the late 1630s on: cultivators were coaxed back with fair, written revenue terms, wells and fields were restored, security was re-established, and farming and trade revived. A residence, the **Lal Mahal** ("Red Mansion"), was built in Pune for Jijabai and Shivaji, and it was his childhood home. Dadoji's careful, honest administration is well attested.

**Story (legend):** The dramatic details of the **curse** — that enemies ran a *donkey-drawn plough* over Pune and drove in an iron bar to curse it forever — and of **Jijabai breaking the curse by re-ploughing the land with a golden plough** are part of the much-loved *tradition* about Pune's rebirth, not events we can confirm from records. They're told here as the famous stories they are. What is solid, and what matters, is the real thing underneath them: a ruined town brought deliberately back to life by people who refused to be afraid of it.

*Marathi words in this chapter:* **Lal Mahal** (the "Red Mansion," Shivaji's childhood home in Pune).
