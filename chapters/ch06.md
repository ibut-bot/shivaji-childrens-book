# Chapter 6 — Dadoji's Lessons

The first thing Dadoji Konddev gave Shivaji, when it was time to begin his real schooling, was not a sword.

It was an account book.

We do not have that book, or any list of the lessons the old administrator set the boy through those Pune years. But we can see the shape of them clearly enough, because the whole rest of Shivaji's life is the proof of what he was taught — and it began, the records suggest, with the unglamorous things. Letters and figures. The names of villages and the boundaries of fields. How much grain a piece of land could bear in a good year, and how much mercy it needed in a bad one.

A boy who had been dreaming of horses and blades did not, we may safely guess, leap for joy at a ledger. But Dadoji Konddev was not a man you argued with twice.

"You will get your sword," is the kind of thing the old man was known for saying, dry as dust. "A sword is easy. Any fool can swing one. *This*—" and here he would have tapped the rows of figures "—this is the hard part. This is how a country is actually held."

---

There were, in truth, two educations going on at once, and Dadoji believed in both.

The first was the one Shivaji had been impatient for. He learned to ride until the horse was less an animal he sat on than a part of his own body. He learned the sword, the spear, the bow; he learned to wrestle in the red earth until he was lean and quick and hard to knock down. The men who taught him were Maval fighters, hill-bred and tough, and they did not go easy on the master's son, because the master had told them not to.

But the second education was the one Dadoji cared about more, and it took place over ledgers and land-maps and the endless small business of running a jagir. How were taxes set? Fairly — written down, fixed, so that a farmer knew exactly what he owed and no one could squeeze him for more. What happened when a powerful village headman seized a poorer man's field, or kept for himself the share that belonged to the people who actually did the digging? Then the powerful man was called to account, rank or no rank — because a rule that bent for the strong was not a rule at all.

Shivaji watched the old man do this, season after season — watched him sit and listen to farmers and weighers and complainers, and untangle who was owed what, and make it come out fair. And slowly the boy understood the thing Dadoji was really teaching him, underneath the figures.

*Governing well is harder than fighting well.* Anyone brave can win a battle. It takes something rarer — patience, fairness, an eye that will not look away — to run a country so that the smallest person in it can sleep without fear.

---

The lessons did not stay indoors.

Shivaji rode out into the country his family was now responsible for — the *Maval*, the folded hill-and-valley land west of Pune — and he learned it the way he had once learned the campfires on the plain: completely, hungrily, one detail at a time. Every goat track and stream-crossing. Every steep place where a few could hold off many. Every village tucked in its fold of the hills, and — more important to him than any map — the people in them.

He did not ride among them as a little lord to be bowed to. He rode among them as a boy who asked questions and actually listened to the answers: how the harvest had been, whether the well held, who was treating them well and who was not. They were farmers and herders and hillmen, the *Mavalas*, poor and proud and nobody's idea of important. The great courts of the Deccan did not know their names. But Shivaji learned them, and they learned his, and something began to grow up between the master's son and the people of those hills that had nothing to do with rank and would, one day, change everything.

His mother had taught him *why* a ruler exists — to be a roof over ordinary people. Dadoji was teaching him *how* — the patient, unromantic craft of actually doing it. And the hills were teaching him *where*, and *for whom*.

---

It made him, by the time he was nearing his teens, a strange and rather formidable boy.

He could ride and fight with anyone his age and most who were older. But he could also read a revenue roll, spot a crooked account, and tell you exactly which family in which valley had been wronged by which official, and what fairness would look like put right. He had the body of a young soldier and the eye of a young magistrate, and burning underneath both was the thing that had lit in him on the wall at Shivneri, watching a village burn: a sense of fairness that had now been handed real tools.

The guarding game had grown up. The wooden sword had become a real one, and the pretend village had become a real country full of real people — and the boy who used to stand in front of a pile of firewood announcing *nobody gets past me to the village* was quietly, methodically, being turned into someone who might actually be able to mean it.

He was not the only boy in those hills, though. Out in the Maval valleys were others his own age — farmers' sons, herders' sons, quick and tough and bored and looking for something to believe in.

It was only a matter of time before they found each other.

### What We Know

**True:** Shivaji's upbringing at Pune was genuinely a *double* one. Under **Jijabai** and **Dadoji Konddev**, he was trained both in the military arts — riding, swordsmanship, and the rest — and, unusually, in the practical work of **administration and governance**: revenue and land records, fair taxation, and the settling of disputes. Dadoji was known for honest, careful management of the jagir, including holding powerful local men to account, and Shivaji grew up steeped in it. He also came to know the **Maval** hill-country and its people intimately in these years — knowledge of the terrain, and bonds with the hardy Maval folk, that would matter enormously later.

**Story:** We have no record of specific lessons, conversations, or a single "first day with the account book," so those details here are drawn as a *representative* picture of a well-documented kind of education — not as particular events. Dadoji's lines are illustrative of his known role and character, not recorded quotes. What is solid is the shape: a boy raised to be as much a fair administrator as a fighter.
