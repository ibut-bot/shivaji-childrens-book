# Chapter 18 — The Forest Kingdom

For ten years, Shivaji had been adding forts to his map the way a boy adds stones to a wall. Torna. Rajgad. Kondhana. Purandar. One valley at a time, one clever bargain or one quiet night-climb at a time, his little kingdom had grown.

But there was one place his finger always came to rest on, and could never cross.

It was not a fort. It was a forest.

On the western edge of his world, where the hills crowded together and the land tilted down toward the far-off sea, lay a wild, drowned-green country of ravines and rivers and trees grown so thick that a man could lose the sun at noon. It was called *Javli*. And whoever held Javli held every road that ran west to the Konkan and the sea, and south into the rich country beyond.

"It is like a gate," Shivaji told his companions, tapping the green smudge on the charcoal map. "A locked gate across the whole bottom of the map. Everything we want is on the other side of it. And we do not have the key."

Beyond that gate lay the things a kingdom needs to truly live. The Konkan ports, where ships came in from across the world. The salt and the trade and the long sea-edge that no hill-fort could give him. A kingdom penned in the high hills, with no road to the water, was a kingdom that could be squeezed slowly to death whenever its enemies chose. Shivaji knew it. And Javli, that drowned-green tangle of ravines, sat square across every path that led down to it.

"We have grown as far as we can grow," he said, "until that forest opens."

---

Javli had a keeper, and the keeper was an old and proud Maratha clan called the **Mores**.

For generations the Mores had ruled their forest from its shadows, lords of Javli in all but crown, their chief carrying the grand inherited title of **Chandrarao**. They held the place under the flag of Bijapur — the Sultan was glad enough to let someone else govern a jungle he could never have governed himself — and in return the Mores answered to no one and let no one in. Armies that marched into Javli did not march out. The forest swallowed them. The Mores knew every game-trail and stream-crossing and hidden clearing, and a stranger knew none of them, and that was the whole of their power.

They were, in short, exactly like Shivaji.

That was the thing that gnawed at him. The Mores were not some distant sultan's foreign soldiers. They were Maratha hillmen, sons of the same Sahyadris, who knew their mountains the way his Mavalas knew theirs. By every right they should have been his brothers in the cause.

Instead they were the one wall he could not climb.

---

So he tried the way he always tried first.

He wrote to them. Not a threat — an offer. He wrote to Chandrarao More as one Maratha to another, and the letter was warm and it was honest. *We are the same people,* it said, in the way Shivaji would have meant it. *Why should the sons of these hills spend their strength serving a sultan in his far-off city, holding the door shut against their own? Open it. Stand with us. There is a thing being built here — a* Swarajya*, a country of our own — and there is a place in it for Javli and for the Mores. Let us build it together.*

It was the offer of brotherhood. He sent it, and he waited.

The reply, when it came, was not warm.

---

Chandrarao More's answer was a slap.

It was proud, and it was mocking, and it was meant to wound. The chief of Javli wrote back as a man writes to an upstart who has forgotten his place.

*You call yourself a raja?* the reply sneered, in the way the Mores would have meant it. *A raja is something the Padshah makes. The Sultan made me Chandrarao — my title is real, my crown is real, given by a king. And you? You made yourself. You raised your own flag with no one's leave and you call a handful of stolen hill-forts a kingdom. That is not a king. That is a thief who has not yet been caught.*

And then, because cruelty was not enough without a dare attached, the letter ended with one:

*Javli has eaten bigger men than you, boy. If you think you can take it — come. Come into the forest and see how far you get.*

Shivaji read it through to the end. His companions watched his face and saw it go very still.

Tanaji could not hold his tongue. "Let me answer that letter," he growled. "I'll need a sword to write it."

"No," Shivaji said quietly. He folded the page along its creases, slowly, the way his mother had once folded a letter that frightened her without letting her hands shake. "Not yet."

---

He did not give the order that day. Or the next.

Because this was not Torna, where the garrison was forty sleepy men and a faded flag. This was not Kondhana, where a tired old killedar set his key on the table for a cup of tea and a fair price. This was the hardest thing Shivaji had faced since he was old enough to choose — harder, in its way, than a battle.

For ten years he had been proud of *how* he won. *We always chose the other way first,* he had told his band. *Words are cheaper than funerals.* He had taken forts by patience, by cleverness, by letting frightened men walk home alive. He had built a kingdom and kept his hands, by the rough measure of his hard century, remarkably clean. The people loved him for it. He loved himself for it.

And Javli was going to cost him that.

There was no clever trick that opened a forest. There was no sleepy garrison to surprise, no quarrel among brothers to lean on, no key anyone would set on any table. The Mores would not be talked out of Javli and they would not be frightened out of it. The only way through that gate was to break it — against fellow Marathas, in their own dark trees, in a fight that would not be quick and would not be gentle and might not, when the histories were written, look clean at all.

He sat with that for a long time.

"If we do this," he said at last, half to his companions and half to himself, "we cannot pretend it is something it is not. This is not justice for a wronged farmer's daughter. This is not a tired old fort that wanted a better master. These are stubborn, proud men who simply stand where I need to walk, and I am going to make them move, and some of them are going to die for standing there."

The fire cracked. No one spoke.

"So I will not lie to myself about it, and I will not let anyone sing about it afterward as though it were glorious." He looked up. "But it has to be done. As long as Javli is shut, *Swarajya* is just a handful of hills with the sea forever out of reach. The cause is bigger than my clean hands. That is the worst thing I have ever had to believe, and I believe it."

---

Jijabai found him later, still by the dead fire, the folded letter in his hand.

"You are troubled," she said. It was not a question.

"I have always known what kind of king I did *not* want to be," he said. "A storm. Something that falls on people. You taught me that on a rampart before I could walk." He turned the letter over. "Javli is the first time being the other kind — a roof, the good kind — and being the storm are the same direction. To protect everything we are building, I have to march into another man's home and take it from him by force. I do not know how to make those two things agree."

Jijabai was quiet for a moment.

"They do not always agree," she said. "That is the hardest part of a crown, and you have found it younger than most. There will be days when there is no clean road, only a wrong one and a worse one, and you will have to choose, and live with it, and not lie about it afterward." She put her hand over his. "You chose the cause over your own comfort. Remember that it cost you something. The day it stops costing you something is the day you have become the storm."

He nodded slowly. It was not comfort, exactly. But it was the truth, and from his mother the truth had always been the only comfort that lasted.

---

In the days that followed, the camp changed.

Bahirji Naik's quiet travellers drifted west and came back with the shapes of things — which trails the Mores watched, where the streams could be crossed, where the great forest thinned just enough for armed men to pass. Maps were drawn and burned and drawn again. The Mavalas, who had spent their whole lives in country much like Javli, sharpened blades and said little, the way men do before a hard thing.

Shivaji gave no speeches. He had said the only true thing there was to say, by the fire, and he did not dress it up now.

He simply looked west, to the green wall on the edge of his map, where a proud old clan waited in the dark under the trees and dared him to come.

It was January. The streams were low and the forest was as crossable as it would ever be.

He gave the order.

### What We Know

**True:** **Javli** was a real and strategically vital forested principality in the western Sahyadris, ruled by the powerful **More clan**, whose hereditary chief held the title **Chandrarao**. The Mores governed Javli under the authority of **Bijapur**, and their control of that almost impassable forest country blocked Shivaji's expansion toward the **Konkan** and the sea — which is exactly why he needed it. There really was a breakdown of relations: Shivaji sought to bring Javli over to his side, the Mores refused and remained loyal to Bijapur, and the exchange between them turned hostile, with the More chief replying defiantly and contemptuously (the boast that *his* title was given by the Padshah while Shivaji had crowned himself is drawn from that documented arrogance). The campaign that followed began in **January 1656**.

**The hard part — told honestly:** Of everything in this book, the conquest of Javli is the episode that sits least comfortably, and we will not pretend otherwise. The Mores were not foreign invaders or cruel oppressors of the poor — they were fellow Marathas defending their own home, and Shivaji took it from them by force because he needed the ground they stood on. The way it ended was genuinely dark: in the course of the conflict **Chandrarao More was killed** — by some accounts struck down by Shivaji's envoys during the very dealings between them — and members of the More family were later captured and harshly dealt with. Historians have debated this for centuries: was it cold treachery, or the brutal arithmetic of survival in a brutal age, when Shivaji's small movement could not afford an enemy at its back holding the only road west? Both things can be true. A great cause does not make every act done in its name clean, and the best histories of Shivaji do not hide this one. We have shown him knowing that himself — choosing the cause over his own clean hands, and refusing to lie about the cost. That refusal is the most important thing in the chapter.

**Story:** The letters are dramatised — the *attitudes* in them are documented, the exact wording is ours. The fireside conversation and the talk with Jijabai are imagined, framed to carry the real moral weight of the decision, not as recorded events.

*Words in this chapter:* **Chandrarao** (the hereditary title of the More chiefs of Javli); **Konkan** (the coastal lowland strip between the Sahyadri mountains and the Arabian Sea).
