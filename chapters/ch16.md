# Chapter 16 — The Cleverest Letter

For three days after the battle, Shivaji did not look at a single fort. He looked at a map.

Not his own charcoal map of the hills, the one he had drawn by a fire all those years ago. A bigger one. The whole Deccan, spread across half of India — and on it, three giants, not one.

There was Bijapur in the south, the sultanate his family served and defied. There was Golconda away to the east, rich with diamonds. And there, pressing down from the north like a slowly closing hand, was the greatest of them all: the Mughal empire, the mightiest power in the world, whose emperor in distant Delhi ruled more people and more gold than Shivaji could properly imagine.

He had beaten Fateh Khan. He could not beat *that.* Not with every hillman in the Sahyadris.

But he had noticed something, staring at the map for three days. The three giants did not trust each other. They watched each other far more nervously than any of them watched a boy in the hills. And a small man caught between giants, Shivaji was beginning to understand, does not survive by being strong.

He survives by making the giants nervous in the right direction.

---

"I'm going to write a letter," he announced.

Tanaji, who had been hoping the answer to his father's imprisonment would involve swords, looked deeply unimpressed. "Another letter. To Bijapur."

"No." Shivaji's eyes had the gleam in them now. "To the Mughals. To the prince who governs the Deccan for the emperor — Murad Bakhsh, son of the great Shah Jahan himself."

The room went very quiet.

"Shivaji." Tanaji said it carefully, the way you speak to a friend standing too near a cliff. "The *Mughals.* You've spent your whole life saying you'll bow to no foreign throne. And now you want to write to the biggest foreign throne there is and — what? Beg?"

"Not beg. *Offer.*" He was already pacing. "I'm going to write the prince the most loyal, most respectful letter he has ever received. I'm going to suggest, ever so humbly, that a man like me — with my hills, my forts, my men — might one day make a very useful servant to a great empire. That if the Mughals were ever to want friends in the Deccan, the Bhonsle family would be worth having."

"But you don't *mean* it."

"Of course I don't mean it." He stopped pacing. "But think, Tanaji. Think who reads that letter next. Not the Mughals — the *spies.* Bijapur's spies are everywhere, and the moment they hear that the troublesome Bhonsle boy is exchanging warm letters with the Mughal prince, what does Bijapur suddenly realise?"

Slowly, slowly, Tanaji began to grin.

"That if they hurt your father," he said, "they don't just make *you* angry."

"They shove my whole family — my forts, my hills, all of it — straight into the arms of the one empire they're most terrified of." Shivaji nodded. "Bijapur can afford an angry boy. They cannot afford handing the Mughals a foothold in these mountains. Killing my father stops being clever and starts being suicide." He spread his hands. "I don't have to fight Bijapur. I just have to make them do the arithmetic."

---

So he wrote it — and it was, even by his standards, a small masterpiece of saying one thing and meaning another.

Every line of it glowed with loyalty and respect for the mighty Mughal prince. Every line hinted, delicately, at how very valuable the friendship of the Bhonsles might someday be. Nowhere did it say *free my father or I defect.* It did not have to. The threat was not in the ink; the threat was in the simple fact that the letter *existed* — that the boy in the hills was now a name the Mughal court knew, a door standing very slightly open.

When it was finished he folded it, and warmed the wax, and pressed his own seal into it — the Sanskrit seal, the one that shone *for the welfare of the people* — and sent it north, into the territory of the most dangerous men in India.

It was a strange and dangerous game to be playing. He was reaching out, for the very first time, to the great empire of the north — the giant that would, one distant day, become the deadliest enemy of his entire life. He could not have known that yet. But some part of him felt the size of what he was touching, and was sober about it even as he sealed the letter.

For now, the giant served his purpose simply by *noticing* him.

---

And it worked. Of course it worked.

Far to the south, in the marble court of Bijapur, the mood around Shahaji's imprisonment changed almost overnight. The same lords who had been so pleased to chain him began, suddenly, to look nervous. Word had reached them — as Shivaji had known it would — that the Bhonsle boy was being courted by the Mughals, and was perhaps courting them back.

The arithmetic was brutally simple, and Bijapur did it fast. A dead Shahaji was worth nothing. A freed Shahaji, sent home grateful, might keep his dangerous sons loyal to Bijapur and *out* of Mughal hands. Suddenly the most sensible thing the Sultan could do was the very thing Shivaji wanted: let the prisoner go.

In the spring, after long months in chains, Shahaji Bhonsle walked out of his imprisonment a free man.

He had not been freed by an army. No fort had been stormed for him, no battle fought at his cell door. He had been freed by a folded piece of paper, and by a son clever enough to frighten an empire's enemies with the mere *suggestion* of a friendship he never intended to keep.

---

It was not free, of course. Nothing ever was.

The price of Shahaji's life was paid in stone. To seal the peace and prove he would behave, Shivaji had to hand back one of his hard-won forts — and the one Bijapur demanded was **Kondhana**, the great fortress he had won so beautifully, without a single drop of blood, over a cup of tea with a tired old soldier. He had to give it back.

He did it without hesitating, and he did not let anyone see it cost him anything. But it cost him. He thought of old Siddi Amber and the iron key on the table, and he handed the mountain back to Bijapur, and he made himself a quiet promise that he did not say out loud: *not forever. I am giving you back this stone for my father's life, and that is a good trade, the best I ever made. But not forever.*

There was a second part to the price, and in its way it was heavier than the fort. He had to *promise to sit still.* To stop taking forts. To stop being trouble. To be, for a while, the quiet and obedient young nobleman Bijapur wished he were.

For a boy with a vow burning a hole in his chest, it was the hardest price of all.

---

But his father was free. That was the thing that mattered, and he held onto it.

Somewhere far in the south, Shahaji — the father he had met only a handful of times, the voice from the folded letters — was a free man again, and he knew exactly how it had happened. He knew that the small son he had barely been there to raise had grown into someone who could reach across the whole of India and pluck him out of a sultan's prison without drawing a sword. Whatever else lay unspoken between the absent father and the son who had freed him, that knowledge sat there now, warm and certain: *the boy could do impossible things.*

The lion had reached out to hurt him through his father, and Shivaji had turned the blow aside with a single sheet of paper.

Now came the hard part — harder, in its way, than any battle. He had promised to sit quietly. And so, with forts still to win and a whole kingdom still only a vow, the most restless young man in the Deccan was going to have to learn the one thing he had never once been any good at.

Waiting.

### What We Know

**True:** After failing to free his father by force, Shivaji really did secure **Shahaji's release through clever diplomacy** rather than war. In **1649** he wrote to the Mughal prince **Murad Bakhsh** (a son of Emperor **Shah Jahan**, then governing the Deccan for the Mughals), professing loyalty and offering future service — a move that alarmed Bijapur, which did not want to push the powerful Bhonsle family into Mughal hands. The Mughals acknowledged Shivaji, Bijapur felt the squeeze, and **Shahaji was released in 1649.** The price was real: Shivaji **gave back the fort of Kondhana** (his brother gave up Bangalore), along with a period of keeping quiet. This is a genuine, documented example of Shivaji using diplomacy and the rivalry between great powers as a weapon.

**Story:** The conversations (with Tanaji) and Shivaji's private thoughts are an **imagined dramatisation** of this well-documented diplomatic episode. The exact wording of his letter to Murad Bakhsh does not survive; we've described its clever spirit, not quoted it. We've simplified a tangled three-way political situation (Bijapur, the Mughals, and Golconda all manoeuvring) down to its heart. And there is a quiet piece of foreshadowing here that is simply true: this was Shivaji's **first real contact with the Mughal empire** — the power that, decades later, would become the greatest adversary of his life.
