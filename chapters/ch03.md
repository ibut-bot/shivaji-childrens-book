# Chapter 3 — Jijabai's Stories

"But why didn't Rama just *keep* the kingdom?" Shivaji demanded. "It was his. They said so. The throne was his."

The lamp had burned low. It was long past the hour when a six-year-old should have been asleep, and Jijabai had reached the part of the story where the prince Rama, who should have been crowned king that very morning, instead bowed to his father's promise and walked away into the forest for fourteen years.

"It was his," Jijabai agreed. "And he gave it up, to keep his father's word. That is what made him great, Shiva. Not the crown. Anyone can sit on a crown. Holding to what is *right* when it costs you everything — that is the hard thing. That is the kingly thing."

Shivaji turned this over with his usual fierce attention, frowning at the shadows the lamp threw on the wall. To him the shadows were never just shadows. They were the dark forest, and the ten-headed demon king, and the great bow that no ordinary man could lift.

"Tell the part where he fights Ravana."

"Tomorrow."

"Tell the part where Bhavani gives him the weapons."

"*Tomorrow,*" she said, and pinched out the flame. But she sat a while in the dark with her hand on his hair, because she could hear that he was not really asking for the fighting. He was asking the question underneath all the others, the one he asked a dozen ways and she answered a dozen ways and he never quite stopped asking.

*Where is a king like that now?*

---

The stories were not only Rama's.

On feast days, and on the dark nights when the wind came howling off the hills and a small boy needed a bigger courage than his own, Jijabai told him about the Goddess. About Bhavani, who rode a tiger and carried a sword in each of her many hands, who was gentle as a mother to those who needed protecting and terrible as a storm to those who preyed on them. The demons in those stories were strong, and proud, and sure no one could touch them.

They were always wrong.

In the best of the tales the goddess came down herself, and Shivaji could see the whole of it while his mother spoke — the sky splitting open, the tiger's eyes like two lamps, the demon's army of thousands suddenly not nearly enough. He held his breath at the same place every time, though he knew exactly how it ended. The not-knowing was never the point. The point was that somewhere, in the oldest part of the world, strength had once stood on the side of the weak. And a thing that had happened once was a thing that could happen.

"She gives her sword," Jijabai would say, "to whoever is brave enough to lift it for the right reason. Not for gold. Not for a throne. For the ones who cannot lift it themselves."

"Has anyone ever lifted it?"

"Heroes. Long ago."

"Could someone lift it *now*?"

And here Jijabai would always look at her son for a moment longer than the question needed — at the black hair and the stubborn chin and the eyes that took the whole world apart to see how it worked — and she would say, carefully, "If someone were brave enough. And good enough. And patient enough to wait until he was strong. Yes. Someone could."

Shivaji did not know why his mother's voice went so quiet on those words. He only knew that he liked the feeling they gave him, like standing at the edge of the high wall with the whole country spread out below.

---

By day, the stories climbed down off the wall and into the dusty corner of the fort where the children played.

There were a dozen of them — the cook's boys, the gate-guard's daughter, the children of soldiers and servants — and somehow, without anyone quite deciding it, they had all become Shivaji's. Not because he was the lady's son; he never once said it, and would have been puzzled if you had pointed it out. It was simpler than that. He knew the best games, and the best game was always the same one.

"You're the village," he told the smallest ones, lining them up behind a low wall of stacked firewood. "Stay behind me. You two — you're the raiders. You come from over there." He hefted the wooden sword the carpenter had cut him, the one he carried everywhere now. "And nobody gets past me to the village. That's the rule."

"Why do you always guard it?" complained a bigger boy who wanted the sword. "Why can't I have a turn guarding?"

Shivaji looked honestly puzzled. "Because somebody has to," he said.

They played it a hundred times that year, on that dusty patch beneath the ramparts — the guarding game, the only one he never grew tired of. None of them knew they were practising for anything. These particular children would scatter soon enough, when the family left the fort for good. But the game would not leave *him.* He would play it again in a few years, in the hills above Pune, with rougher boys whose names the whole country would one day learn.

---

Then, one ordinary afternoon, the stories and the world came together, and it was nothing like he had imagined.

He was on the rampart, where he always was, when the smoke began. It rose from a village in the valley below — not the friendly grey smoke of cooking fires, but black, fast, wrong. He watched, not understanding, as small figures came pouring out of the houses and scattered into the fields. And then he saw the horsemen.

There were not many of them. That was the part that lodged in him and never came out — that it took so *few*. A dozen riders, maybe, broken off from some passing army, sweeping through a village that had done nothing to anyone. They drove off the thin cattle. They took what little grain the famine had left. A house caught, and then another, and the black smoke climbed into the clean sky, and the small figures in the fields did not fight, because there was nothing to fight with, and nowhere to go.

"Aai," Shivaji whispered. *Mother.* He had gone very white. "Aai, make them stop. Someone make them stop."

Jijabai had come to the wall. She did not gasp, and she did not pull him away, though every part of her wanted to. She put one hand flat between his shoulder blades and made herself stand there with him and watch, because she had decided long ago that she would not raise a boy who learned to look away.

"I cannot make them stop," she said quietly. "Neither can the killedar. Neither can anyone on this rock today."

"*Why?*" And it came out of him as something close to rage — the first real rage of his life, hot and helpless and far too big for his small body. "They're stealing! They're *burning* it! Where are the soldiers? Where is the king? Isn't there a king who's supposed to stop this?"

"There are three kings," Jijabai said. "You have seen their armies. And not one of them is awake to a burning village. To them, those people are too small to see."

Shivaji's hands were gripping the cold stone exactly as his mother's had gripped it, on this same wall, before he was born. Below, the village burned, and the horsemen finished and rode away unhurried, afraid of no one.

"Then the kings are wrong," he said.

"Yes."

"All of them."

"Yes."

"Someone should make a kingdom where this *doesn't happen.*" He turned and looked up at her, his face streaked and furious and absolutely certain. "Someone should."

And Jijabai, who had waited six years for a sentence very like this one, did not smile and did not weep, though she felt both rising in her like a tide. She only crouched down to his height, and held his shoulders, and looked into the eyes she had asked the goddess to give courage on the day they were born.

"Yes," she said. "Someone should. Remember that you said it."

---

That night he could not sleep, and for once he did not ask for the fighting part of any story.

"The village," he said into the dark. "Will they build it again?"

"Some of it. The ones who lived will go somewhere and start over. People are braver than kings, Shiva. They always start over."

He was quiet so long she thought he had finally gone under. Then: "When I'm big, will I be strong enough to lift Bhavani's sword?"

Jijabai lay in the dark and chose her words the way the killedar chose his footing on the steep path — slowly, and without a single wasted step.

"Strong enough is something you build," she said. "A day at a time. A wall is only stones, set one on another, by someone who does not stop. You make yourself the same way. And one day you look up, and you are a fort." She drew the shawl over his shoulder. "Sleep now. Building starts in the morning."

He slept.

And far to the south, beyond the fires and the burned villages and the three sleeping kings, Shivaji's father was fighting the last battle of a long, losing war — a war that was about to end, and send a letter north, and change the shape of everything. But that is the next part of the story.

---

### What We Know

**True:** Jijabai is remembered above all as the person who shaped Shivaji's mind and heart. The tradition is very strong that she raised him on the **Ramayana**, the **Mahabharata**, and tales of the goddess **Bhavani**, and that she taught him to see a ruler's duty as the *protection of ordinary people* rather than the grabbing of wealth and thrones. Devotion to Bhavani stayed with Shivaji all his life. And the Deccan of his childhood really was a place where small bands of raiders could burn a village while great kings looked the other way.

**Story:** This exact evening — these lines of dialogue, this particular raid watched from the wall — is dramatised. We can't know the words Jijabai used or the day Shivaji first felt that flash of anger at injustice. But every part of it grows from what is genuinely remembered: a remarkable mother, the stories she chose, and a boy who grew up believing the world could be made fairer than it was.

*Marathi words in this chapter:* **Aai** (mother).
