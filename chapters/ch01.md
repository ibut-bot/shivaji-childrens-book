# Chapter 1 — The Road to Shivneri

The horsemen would not let the lamps be lit.

"No fire," the captain said, riding close to the curtained litter. "Not until we are off the plain."

Inside the swaying *palki*, Jijabai pressed a hand flat against the wooden frame to steady herself and looked out through the gap in the curtain. The Deccan lay dark and flat under a sky pricked with cold stars. Somewhere far to the south a red glow smudged the night — a village burning, or a camp, she could not tell. She had learned, this past year, that it did not much matter which. Fire on the horizon meant the same thing everywhere now. Move. Keep moving.

"How far?" she asked.

"By dawn we'll see the hill," the captain said. "By midday, the gate."

She let the curtain fall. The child shifted inside her, a slow turning, and she breathed out and waited for it to settle. *Soon,* she told it silently. *Be patient a little longer. I am taking you somewhere they cannot reach.*

She did not say the rest, even to herself. That she did not know if anywhere was truly out of reach. That three armies were loose in the land — the sultan of Bijapur's men, the sultan of Ahmadnagar's men, and the great Mughal host pressing down from the north — and that her husband was somewhere among them with a sword in his hand, fighting for a king who might not last the season.

Shahaji. She closed her eyes and saw him as he had been at the parting, tall on his grey horse, his armour dusty, trying to make light of it the way he always did.

"You worry too much," he had told her, and grinned. "I'll send word at every full moon."

"You'll forget by the second."

"Then you'll know I'm winning, and too busy to write."

But under the joking his eyes had been steady, and serious, and he had taken her hands and said the true thing at last. "Go to Shivneri. It's the strongest fort in these hills — seven gates, walls a goat couldn't climb, and water that never runs dry. Our child will be born inside stone, Jija. Born safe. Whatever happens to me."

*Whatever happens to me.* She had not let him see how those words landed. A wife of a soldier learns not to flinch where he can see it.

---

Dawn came grey and then gold, and with the gold came the hill.

Jijabai pushed the curtain all the way back to look at it. Shivneri rose out of the morning mist like something a giant had set down and forgotten — a great shoulder of black rock, sheer on every side, with a green crown of trees at the very top and, just visible, the pale line of a wall running along the edge of the sky. Birds wheeled around its cliffs. It looked less like a fort that men had built than like a thing the earth itself had grown to keep something precious.

"There," the captain breathed, and even he, who must have seen it a hundred times, sounded glad.

The road up was not a road at all but a climbing track that doubled back on itself again and again, clinging to the rock. They left the *palki* at the foot and Jijabai walked — slowly, one hand on a soldier's offered arm, refusing the chair they wanted to carry her in.

"My lady should not climb," the captain fretted.

"Your lady has been sitting for four days," she said. "Let me meet this fort on my own feet."

They passed through the first gate, and then she understood her husband's promise. The gate was not one gate but a trap of gates — a narrow turn of stone where a handful of defenders could hold off an army, then another above it, and another, each one set at an angle so no charging enemy could get a straight run at it. Spikes studded the great wooden doors. Murder-holes watched from above. By the time they climbed through the last of the seven, Jijabai's legs ached and her heart was strangely light.

*Seven gates,* she thought. *They would have to take all seven to reach you. And they will not.*

At the top, the world opened. The whole tableland of the fort spread before her — barracks and grain stores, a great rock-cut water tank already catching the morning light, and beyond the walls, in every direction, the brown and folded hills of the *Maval* country rolling away to the west like a frozen sea.

An old man was waiting for her, lean and grey-bearded, with the bearing of a soldier and the careful eyes of a man who had buried more friends than he could count. He pressed his palms together.

"I am the *killedar* of Shivneri," he said. "The keeper of this fort. Shahaji's word came ahead of you, my lady. Everything here is yours — the walls, the water, the watch. You will want for nothing."

"I will want for one thing only," Jijabai said. "News. When it comes, good or bad, you bring it to me yourself. You do not soften it. Promise me that."

The old killedar looked at her a moment longer than was polite, as if revising something in his mind. Then he bowed, and this time it was not merely courtesy.

"I promise it," he said.

---

The waiting was the hardest part, and the longest.

Winter deepened over the hills. The full moons came and, true to his word and against her teasing, Shahaji's messages came with most of them — a few lines scratched on a scrap of paper, carried by a tired rider who had ridden through country no sane man crossed alone. *Still alive. Still yours. The war goes badly, but I go on.* She read each one a hundred times and kept them folded in a small brass box.

Below the fort, the land suffered. From the ramparts she watched the roads, and the roads were never empty now. Lines of people trailed along them — whole villages on the move, carrying children and copper pots and not much else, driven out by the armies or by the hunger that walked behind the armies. The rains had failed. The grain was running out across the whole Deccan, and the great ones who made the wars did not seem to notice, or did not care, that it was the small ones who starved.

Jijabai watched them from the safe stone height, and something in her hardened and grew clear.

"They have no one," she said one evening, to no one, to the wind.

But the killedar was near, and heard. "My lady?"

"All those people on the road." She did not turn from the wall. "Kings make war and farmers pay for it. Has it always been so?"

"In my lifetime, always," the old man said quietly.

"Then men have made it so," said Jijabai, "and men can unmake it." She laid both hands on the cold parapet. "A king should be a roof, killedar. Something the small people can stand under when the sky turns to fire. Not another storm."

The killedar said nothing. But he remembered it. Years later, he would tell people he had heard the beginning of everything spoken on that wall, by a woman with a child still inside her, and not known it.

---

There was a temple on the fort.

It was small and old, tucked among the trees near the highest point, and inside it sat the goddess the hill was named for — Shivai, lady of the place, carved in dark stone and smoothed by the touch of a thousand hands. Jijabai went to her every day as the time drew near. She did not ask the goddess for safety; she had stone walls for that. She asked for something harder.

"Give him courage," she murmured, kneeling, her forehead nearly to the floor. "Not luck. Luck runs out. Courage — and a true heart — and the eyes to see who needs protecting. That is all. Give him that, and I will do the rest myself."

And then, on a cold bright morning in the heart of winter, in the year that the world remembers as 1630, the waiting ended.

It was just before dawn. The killedar was woken by a runner and came at once to the courtyard outside the women's rooms, and there he stood in the dark with his old heart hammering, the way fathers and grandfathers and keepers of forts have always stood, useless and praying, on the wrong side of a closed door.

The first light touched the top of the wall.

And then, thin and furious and absolutely alive, a baby's cry went up into the morning — rang off the stone, climbed the air, carried out over the seven gates and the sleeping hills.

The killedar found that his face was wet. He did not bother to hide it; there was no one watching but the brightening sky.

A little while later they let him in. Jijabai lay pale and triumphant, and in the crook of her arm was a small dark furious creature with a great deal of black hair and two fists already clenched as if he meant to hold on to the whole world and never let go.

"A son," the woman attending whispered. "A strong one. Listen to him."

Jijabai looked down at the small fierce face, and then up, through the doorway, toward the little temple among the trees where the goddess sat in the dark.

"Shivaji," she said. "For the lady of this hill. Shivai's child."

Outside, the sun cleared the eastern hills and poured gold across the ramparts of the strongest fort in the Maval country. The child went on crying — strong, and clear, and unafraid — and somewhere far to the south his father, who did not yet know, fought on through a failing war for a kingdom that was already lost.

But here, born inside stone exactly as he had promised, was the boy who would one day build a better one.

### What We Know

**True:** Shivaji was born on the hill-fort of **Shivneri**, near Junnar, in **February 1630**, while his father, **Shahaji**, served as a warrior in the chaotic wars between the Deccan sultanates and the Mughal Empire. Shivneri really does have a famous series of gates and a rock-cut water cistern, and there really is a temple to the goddess **Shivai** on the fort — most stories agree that Shivaji was named after her. The 1630s truly were years of terrible war and famine across the Deccan, which fell hardest on ordinary farming families.

**Story:** We don't have a record of exactly what Jijabai said or did on the journey, or of the old *killedar* (fort-keeper) — his conversations here are invented to help us picture a real moment. Shivaji's parting words from Shahaji are imagined too. But Jijabai's strength, her devotion, and her belief that a ruler's first duty is to protect ordinary people are very well remembered — and that belief is where our whole story begins.

*Marathi words in this chapter:* **palki** (a covered seat carried on poles), **Maval** (the hill-and-valley country west of Pune), **killedar** (the commander of a fort).
