# Chapter 13 — Three Forts by Wit

"Are we climbing this one in the dark too?" Tanaji asked, eyeing the great green mountain of Kondhana, rubbing his hands together in a way that meant he rather hoped so.

"No," said Shivaji.

"Sneaking, then. Over a wall."

"No."

Tanaji's face fell. "We're not just going to *walk up to the gate.*"

"That," said Shivaji, "is exactly what we're going to do." He was already nudging his pony onto the cart-track that wound up toward the fort, in broad and cheerful daylight, with no army behind him at all. "Bring your best manners. We're going visiting."

---

The fort of **Kondhana** was a giant — three long stone arms of rampart thrown out along a high plateau, sheer cliffs of black basalt falling away beneath walls studded with watchtowers, two great gates with doors thick as a man and bound in iron. Torna had been forgotten and weak. Kondhana was neither. You could lose a thousand men trying to climb those walls, and Shivaji had no intention of losing even one.

Kondhana had something better than a weak wall, anyway. It had a tired old man in charge of it.

His name was **Siddi Amber**, and he had kept that fort for Bijapur for more years than he cared to count — a lean, grizzled commander with a soldier's straight back and eyes that had seen every kind of trouble walk up that cart-track and had stopped being impressed by any of it. He received the famous young rebel in the gatehouse with cool courtesy, and tea, and absolutely no illusions.

"I know who you are, Bhonsle," he said. "I know what you've been doing in these hills. You've come to take my fort." He sipped. "You can't, by the way. Look at my walls."

"I've looked at them," Shivaji said. "They're magnificent. I wouldn't dream of attacking them." He set down his cup. "I came to ask you to give them to me instead."

The old Siddi laughed — a real laugh, surprised out of him. "Did you, now."

---

"Hear me out," Shivaji said, and leaned forward, and the laughter faded as the old man realised the boy was perfectly serious. "How long since Bijapur sent you your pay on time? How long since anyone in that marble court so much as remembered the name of this fort, or yours, except to wonder why it costs them money? You've given them your whole life. What have they given you? Late wages and a cold mountain."

Siddi Amber said nothing, which was itself a kind of answer.

"I'm not asking you to be a traitor," Shivaji went on, more gently. "I'm asking you to be a sensible man. You serve masters who've forgotten you, for a wage that doesn't come, defending a fort against the one person in the Deccan who actually values it. Walk away with honour, and with enough gold to rest easy for the rest of your days, and let me look after Kondhana. Or stay, and defend cold stone for ungrateful men, and we both spend lives we don't need to spend." He spread his hands. "I'd rather we didn't. I have never wanted any of this to cost more than it has to."

There was a long silence in the gatehouse. The wind moved over the great walls that nobody, that day, was going to have to die on.

"You're a strange sort of conqueror," Siddi Amber said at last.

"I'm trying to be," said Shivaji.

The old man looked at him a while longer. Then, slowly, he took the heavy iron key of the main *darwaza* off his belt, weighed it in his hand as though weighing the whole of his long life — and set it on the table between them.

---

Tanaji could not get over it.

"You *talked* him out of it," he kept saying, as they rode away from a fort that was now theirs, its garrison paid and peaceably departed, not one man harmed. "A fort like that. With *words.*"

"Words are cheaper than funerals," Shivaji said. "And a wall you talk your way past is a wall you don't have to repair afterwards. Look back, Tanaji. Look at it." Tanaji looked back at Kondhana, whole and undamaged and flying a new flag. "If we'd stormed it, half of that would be rubble and half of our friends would be under it. Instead we have the fort, *and* the men, *and* the goodwill of an old soldier who'll tell every garrison in these hills that Shivaji Bhonsle keeps his word and doesn't waste lives." He smiled. "That story is worth more than the fort."

---

It became, that season, almost a habit.

There was a fort called **Purandar**, a mighty double-crowned mountain not far off — an upper citadel riding high above a broad walled shoulder, with a smaller sister-fort standing guard at its side. Purandar had belonged for four generations to a family who were old friends of Shivaji's own father, the sons of a fine man lately dead. And that, it turned out, was the trouble: the old keeper was gone, and his sons had fallen to quarrelling among themselves over who should hold the fort now, each brother trusting the others a little less every day.

Into that quarrel, courteous and calm and maddeningly reasonable, walked Shivaji.

He came, he said, only as a friend of the family, to help them settle their dispute. And settle it he did — by talking with each brother, and soothing each brother, and being the one man all of them could agree to trust precisely because he was not one of them. By the time the dust of the argument settled, somehow it was Shivaji's flag, not any brother's, flying over Purandar — and every one of them had thanked him for it.

"I don't fully understand what just happened," Tanaji admitted, on the ride home from *that* one.

"Three brothers who couldn't agree on anything," Shivaji said, "agreed on me. That's all." A third fort, won without a sword leaving its sheath. And there were others, that season and the next — a keeper here who simply liked his odds better on Shivaji's side, a garrison there that opened its gate rather than starve for absent masters. The map in the hills was quietly filling up with his flags, and the strange and wonderful thing was how little blood had paid for any of it.

---

"It won't always be this way," he told them one evening, because he could feel the band getting drunk on how easy it had been, and that frightened him more than any wall. "Hear me. We've been lucky, and we've been clever, and we've taken forts the way I *like* to take them — with a word, with a key on a table, with a man walking home alive to his family instead of being carried there. Remember this feeling. Remember that it can be done this way."

"Why?" asked Yesaji, quietly, who always heard the thing underneath. "Why remember it especially?"

Shivaji was quiet for a moment, looking into the fire.

"Because one day it won't be a tired old man with a key," he said. "One day they'll send someone who isn't tired, and isn't reasonable, and can't be talked around. And on that day there will be blood, ours and theirs, and no clever words will stop it." He looked up. "When that day comes, I want every one of you to remember that we always, *always* chose the other way first. That we spent steel only when they left us nothing else. A king who kills because he must is a hard thing. A king who kills because he *can* is just one more storm." He poked the fire. "We are not going to be a storm."

It was the right thing to say, and a true thing, and it settled them.

But far to the south, in the marble halls he kept writing his polite, lying letters to, the patience he had been counting on was at last running thin. A boy did not pocket fort after fort after fort — by sword or by smile, it made no difference which — without the great men noticing that the map had changed shape. And great men, when they finally notice, do not send tired old keepers with keys.

They send armies. And worse than armies.

But that reckoning was still a little way off. And before it came, Shivaji would lose someone — not to a sword, but to time, and the quiet ordinary grief that comes even to the houses of the brave.

### What We Know

**True:** In the years right after Torna (later 1640s), Shivaji really did rapidly gain a string of important forts around Pune — including **Kondhana** (today's **Sinhagad**), **Purandar**, and **Chakan** — and a great deal of this early expansion came **not from battle but from negotiation, persuasion, and well-judged payments**. Kondhana was genuinely obtained by winning over its Adilshahi commander, **Siddi Amber** (sources say through a mix of persuasion and a payment to the fort-keeper), rather than by assault. **Purandar** had long been held by the family of his father's friend (the Nilkanthrao line), and Shivaji really did gain control by stepping into a **dispute among the keeper's sons.** This preference for winning by wit and sparing lives is one of the most genuine and remarkable things about him.

**Story (compressed):** These gains actually unfolded across **several years**, and Purandar in particular came to Shivaji in stages — the decisive takeover amid the brothers' quarrel was some years later than shown here. We've gathered them into one chapter to show the *pattern,* and the specific conversations (with Siddi Amber, with the brothers) are an illustrative dramatisation of well-attested events. The famous night-battle to retake Kondhana — the one where Tanaji Malusare dies — is a *much* later event and belongs to a later book.

*Words in this chapter:* **darwaza** (a fort gateway/gate).
